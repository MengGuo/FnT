\section{Related Work}
\label{ch01:sec:related_work}

This section reviews the literature needed to position the problems studied in this
monograph. The emphasis is selective rather than encyclopedic. The review starts from
multi-robot task allocation, where missions are usually represented as jobs, visits,
coalitions, and schedules. It then turns to formal methods for task and motion synthesis,
where the mission itself is given a precise temporal semantics. The remaining subsections
follow the assumptions that are gradually relaxed in the later chapters: complete prior
models, exact realizability, centralized coordination, persistent communication, and
static task structure.

\subsection{Task planning and allocation in multi-robot systems}
\label{sec:ch01:rw:allocation}

Task planning and allocation in multi-robot systems concerns how a mission is decomposed
into executable subtasks, how these subtasks are assigned to individual robots or
subteams, and how the resulting activities are coordinated over time. Classical reviews
have established the main taxonomies of multi-robot task allocation and cooperative
planning, with particular emphasis on the distinction between centralized and distributed
coordination \citep{khamis2015multi, torreno2017cooperative}. Recent surveys broaden this
view to market-based, optimization-based, learning-based, and heterogeneous-team settings
\citep{quinton2023market, athira2025systematic, almahmud2026review}. The common abstraction
in this literature is nevertheless still a task, job, or resource claim rather than a
temporal specification with explicit behavioral semantics.

 (I) \emph{Task decomposition and centralized fleet planning}. Centralized
formulations are natural when the planner has access to the full task set, the
capabilities of the fleet, and a common model of cost, precedence, and resource
constraints. They can encode global coupling precisely through mixed-integer, routing,
scheduling, or related combinatorial models, and they are therefore attractive when
heterogeneous capabilities, ordered subtasks, shared resources, or temporal dependencies
must be represented in one decision problem \citep{gini2017multi, ji2021multi,
li2025taskallocation}. Their strength is also their limitation. As the number of robots,
subtasks, and inter-task constraints grows, the resulting search problem becomes difficult
to solve and even harder to re-solve after mission updates. Branch-and-bound and hybrid
exact--heuristic methods alleviate this burden in some regimes, but the scaling issue
remains central \citep{martin2021mrta, athira2025systematic}. Centralized fleet planning is
therefore most effective when the task set is moderately sized, the coupling is
structured, and replanning is relatively infrequent.

 (II) \emph{Distributed allocation and market-based coordination}. Distributed
and market-based methods address the same difficulty by moving part of the decision
process to the robots themselves. In auction-based schemes, robots estimate the utility or
cost of candidate tasks and then negotiate or compete for assignments. This reduces the
need to solve a monolithic team-level optimization problem after every task update and
fits heterogeneous fleets well, because each robot can evaluate its own motion cost,
capability, or uncertainty estimate \citep{zhao2021market, quinton2023market}. A broader
distributed view appears in negotiation-based and constraint-based coordination.
Distributed constraint optimization provides a general language for utility, coupling, and
decentralized decision making \citep{fioretto2018distributed}, while approximate,
metaheuristic, and communication-aware variants enlarge the range of tractable problem
sizes \citep{liu2019multi, ansari2024colossi}. The price is a weaker global view:
optimality guarantees are harder to retain, and feasibility becomes harder to analyze when
the task depends on tight timing, ordering, or collaborative-action constraints.

 (III) \emph{Hierarchical and continual fleet coordination}. Hierarchical
coordination bridges globally informed planning and scalable execution by
decomposing missions across strategic, allocation, and execution layers. This
reduces the dimensionality of each decision problem while retaining enough
global structure for coherent team behavior
\citep{leahy2021scalable, leahy2022fast, luo2024decomposition}. A related line
addresses continual or reactive fleet coordination, where new jobs, requests,
or priorities arise during execution and assignments must be revised online
\citep{dai2024dynamic, street2024rightplace, chen2025realtime}. These methods
are important for deployment, but they often treat tasks as abstract jobs to be
assigned. In the problems studied later in this monograph, feasibility also
depends on temporal ordering, action preconditions, motion constraints, and
execution-time interaction among robots. This difference motivates the shift
from task allocation to formal task and motion synthesis. It also becomes
critical when tasks change online: while many allocation systems insert a new
task by updating a cost matrix, opening an auction, or modifying a schedule, a
temporally specified update may change the language accepted by the task
automaton, the feasible suffixes of an existing plan, or the compatibility
between a local action and a future collaboration. Thus, a small
fleet-management update can have a nonlocal effect on task satisfaction, so the
later chapters do not treat allocation and temporal feasibility as fully
separate stages. Allocation methods provide the fleet-level coordination
background through metrics such as assignment cost, service time, travel
distance, load balance, and throughput, whereas formal methods provide the
language needed to define long-horizon task correctness, including recurrence,
avoidance until service completion, conditional response, and temporally ordered
collaboration.

\subsection{Formal methods for task and motion synthesis}
\label{sec:ch01:rw:formal}

Formal methods provide a principled framework for specifying, verifying, and synthesizing
system behavior from high-level requirements. Their importance in robotics increased once
missions could no longer be reduced to goal reaching or waypoint following. Temporal logic
makes it possible to express ordering, recurrence, safety, and response requirements in a
single semantic language, while automata-based reasoning connects these requirements to
constructive synthesis procedures.

 (I) \emph{Symbolic abstraction and automata-based synthesis}. A foundational
idea is to replace a continuous or hybrid system by a finite symbolic model that preserves
the properties relevant to the specification. Once such a model is available, model
checking and automata-theoretic synthesis can be used to construct behaviors whose traces
satisfy temporal formulas \citep{baier2008principles}. Early abstraction results for
continuous and hybrid systems \citep{alur2000discrete}, approximate bisimulation
\citep{girard2007approximately}, and finite abstractions for linear or piecewise affine
systems \citep{tabuada2006linear, kloetzer2008fully, yordanov2010formal} showed how
continuous dynamics could be connected to finite-state synthesis. Stochastic variants
extended the same logic to controlled Markov decision processes \citep{ding2011mdp}. Across
these settings, the key conceptual shift is the same: task satisfaction is not an external
check on a candidate plan, but the object of synthesis itself.

 (II) \emph{Temporal-logic planning for robotic motion}. In robotics, these ideas
led to the standard abstraction--synthesis--refinement pipeline. A workspace is
partitioned into symbolic regions, feasible transitions are encoded in a finite transition
system, a temporal formula is translated into an automaton, and a plan is synthesized over
the product of the robot model and the task automaton. This architecture was established
in early symbolic robotic planning work \citep{belta2007symbolic, fainekos2009temporal,
bhatia2010sampling} and has since become one of the main formal-methods interfaces to task
and motion planning \citep{zhao2024tampsurvey}. Its main achievement is that surveillance,
sequencing, persistent visitation, and conditional response can be handled within one
logical framework rather than through separate planning heuristics. At the same time, the
pipeline depends strongly on the quality and size of the finite abstraction, which becomes
a recurring issue once the workspace, task labels, or execution assumptions are not fully
known in advance.

 (III) \emph{Integrated task and motion synthesis with actions}. Pure
region-visitation models become restrictive when a mission also contains actions whose
feasibility depends on local conditions, internal robot state, or discrete choices among
alternatives. Planning theory provides action languages and precondition--effect models
for such structure \citep{pednault1989adl, gelfond1998action}, but integrating this
semantics with motion planning under temporal logic is substantially more difficult.
Robotics work on manipulation-oriented temporal planning and motion--action coupling
\citep{he2015towards, guo2013motion} illustrates the need to represent more than region
transitions alone. Some formulations attach actions permanently to locations, while others
treat action propositions as freely switchable labels; both choices can obscure the actual
feasibility of execution when local resources, service behaviors, or collaborative
operations become part of the mission.

 (IV) \emph{Expressiveness, robustness, and scalability limits}. The expressive
power of formal synthesis comes with computational limits. Robustness semantics and
quantitative satisfaction measures make temporal specifications more useful under
uncertainty and model mismatch \citep{fainekos2009robustness, abbas2013probabilistic}, and
recent STL and dynamic-temporal methods address robustness and scalability together
\citep{yang2024stlmpc, buyukkocak2024sequential}. Nevertheless, product-based search and
automaton construction can grow rapidly with specification length, workspace size, and the
number of relevant labels. Many classical methods therefore work best when the environment
is fixed, labels are known in advance, and synthesis can be done largely offline. Those
are precisely the assumptions that begin to fail once the model is incomplete, the nominal
task cannot be realized, or the team must coordinate under limited communication.
This limitation should not be read as a weakness of formal synthesis alone. It is a
consequence of insisting that the task language have a precise semantics and that
synthesized behavior be linked to this semantics by a correctness argument. The central
design question is therefore not whether to use symbolic, optimization-based, or
sampling-based machinery in isolation, but how to choose representations that keep the
semantic structure of the task while avoiding unnecessary growth in the planning object.
This question reappears throughout the monograph in different forms: plan repair in
Chapter~\ref{chap:bottom-up}, partial-order decomposition in Chapter~\ref{chap:top-down},
communication-aware coordination in Chapter~\ref{chap:communication}, and dynamic task
adaptation in Chapter~\ref{chap:uncertainty}.

\subsection{Planning under incomplete models and infeasible specifications}
\label{sec:ch01:rw:incomplete}

Classical temporal synthesis is most effective when the workspace, semantic labels, and
task model are all available before planning begins. Robotic deployment is often less
orderly. The planner may have only partial knowledge of obstacles, traversability, or
task-relevant propositions, and this knowledge may continue to evolve during execution. A
second difficulty appears when the nominal specification cannot be realized over the
available model. The relevant literature therefore relaxes either the assumption of
complete prior knowledge or the expectation of exact task satisfaction.

 (I) \emph{Uncertain and partially known workspace models}. One response to
incomplete knowledge is to encode uncertainty directly in the planning model.
Temporal-logic synthesis over stochastic systems and robust MDP formulations represent
uncertain transitions or observations probabilistically, so that the planner can reason
about satisfaction probability or robust performance rather than exact correctness over a
deterministic abstraction \citep{ding2011mdp, wolff2012robust}. Another response is to
update the synthesis problem online as new information appears. Receding-horizon temporal
synthesis \citep{wongpiromsarn2010receding}, perception-aware temporal planning
\citep{kantaros2022perception}, and no-regret planning in partially known environments
\citep{zhao2025noregret} all weaken the assumption of complete prior knowledge while
preserving formal task semantics. Their common limitation is that uncertainty is usually
built into the model class from the outset. Many robotic deployments also require
correction of a symbolic model that was incomplete or partially inaccurate at the time of
planning.

 (II) \emph{Execution-time plan repair and revision}. This execution-centered
viewpoint leads to plan repair and revision. Instead of resynthesizing from scratch after
every inconsistency, revision methods attempt to preserve as much of the nominal plan as
possible while restoring feasibility or task correctness. Backtracking and iterative
planning methods \citep{livingston2012backtracking, maly2013iterative} capture the
computational value of reusing existing plan structure. Logic-sensitive revision for LTL
motion planning in partially known workspaces \citep{guo2013revising} makes the semantic
issue explicit: if a transition is blocked or a proposition label is updated, the question
is not merely whether another route exists, but whether the remaining behavior still
satisfies the temporal task. These methods remain most developed for single-robot or
weakly coupled settings, where model updates can be localized more easily than in
multi-robot missions with shared resources and collaboration dependencies.

 (III) \emph{Task relaxation and least-violating synthesis}. When the nominal
specification is infeasible, the problem changes from repairing a plan to
interpreting failure. Specification revision and unrealizability analysis
identify which assumptions or temporal clauses cause the failure
\citep{fainekos2011revising, kim2012approximate, raman2011analyzing}, while
least-violating, minimum-violation, and maximally satisfying planning seek the
best admissible compromise relative to the original specification
\citep{tumova2013least, tumova2013minimum, lahijanian2015time,
tumova2014maximally}. This line is especially relevant when requirements have
unequal criticality, because synthesis must prioritize hard and soft
constraints rather than only return satisfiable or unsatisfiable outcomes.
However, much of the work treats adaptation and relaxation with limited
inter-robot coupling. In multi-robot systems, a blocked transition, unavailable
proposition, or failed service action may invalidate not only one robot's route
but also another robot's temporal dependency on that route; similarly, a relaxed
formula may redistribute satisfaction burden across the team or change which
collaborations are worth maintaining. This creates a distinction between
robustness and revision: robustness semantics quantify the margin by which a
behavior satisfies or violates a task, whereas revision asks what should change
when the original behavior or specification can no longer be executed. Both are
needed in open robotic environments, where local updates, task dependencies,
and coordination decisions are coupled rather than independent corrections to
precomputed plans.

\subsection{Temporal-logic coordination under coupled constraints}
\label{sec:ch01:rw:coordination}

Extending temporal-logic synthesis from one robot to a team changes the nature of the
planning problem. The issue is no longer only how to construct one trace that satisfies a
specification, but how to coordinate several traces whose feasibility and correctness are
coupled through space, time, resources, and interaction constraints. The literature has
developed two complementary viewpoints. Top-down methods synthesize team behavior from a
global temporal mission, while bottom-up methods start from locally assigned
specifications and manage the dependencies that arise during execution.

 (I) \emph{Global temporal-task coordination}. In the top-down paradigm, the team
is treated as a composite system and the task is specified over collective behavior. Early
work synthesized deployments and team plans directly from temporal-logic specifications
\citep{kloetzer2010automatic, ding2011automatic}. This viewpoint gives a strong notion of
correctness because logical dependencies and synchronization requirements are represented
at the team level. Subsequent work extended the same paradigm to uncertain travel times,
robustness, task allocation, sampling, capability-aware specifications, and
abstraction-free or decomposition-based planning \citep{ulusoy2012robust,
ulusoy2013optimality, schillinger2018simultaneous, kantaros2020stylus, luo2021abstraction,
luo2022temporal, sahin2019multirobot, cardona2024planning}. The structural limitation is
the size of the joint planning object. The global view captures coupling directly, but it
can grow rapidly with the number of robots, synchronization points, and temporal
dependencies. Hierarchical remedies can delay this growth, but the paradigm remains most
effective when the coordination pattern can be encoded within a centralized or
hierarchically centralized synthesis pipeline.

 (II) \emph{Locally assigned tasks and loosely coupled collaboration}. The
bottom-up viewpoint begins instead with local temporal tasks. Each robot may have its own
role, sensing profile, or action capability, and collaboration appears only where local
activities intersect through shared resources, temporal dependencies, or service requests.
\citet{filippidis2012decentralized, tumova2014receding} showed that decentralized and receding-horizon coordination
from local temporal specifications
need not start from a single team-wide formula. Later work on knowledge transfer,
dependent local tasks, and heterogeneous task-and-motion coordination
made this shift more explicit in~\citet{guo2014distributed, guo2015multi, guo2016task}:
collaboration is triggered by dependency, not imposed as a permanent team-level
synchronization. Hierarchical decomposition methods can partially recover global structure
without returning to a flat centralized product \citep{leahy2021scalable, leahy2022fast,
luo2024decomposition, luo2025hulk}. The resulting technical burden is different from the
top-down case. Correctness must be preserved across distributed decisions, evolving
dependencies, and execution-time modifications of local plans.
The difference between global and local specifications should also be understood as a
difference in where uncertainty enters the problem. In a global task, the planner must
reason about all relevant dependencies before execution or encode how those dependencies
may change. In a local-task formulation, by contrast, some dependencies may be discovered
only when one robot's plan reaches a state that requires another robot's assistance or
information. This makes the local view more flexible, but it also means that coordination
cannot be reduced to a single initial synthesis call. The plan must retain enough semantic
structure to support later negotiation, repair, and verification.


 (III) \emph{Interaction constraints and relative-motion safety}. A further layer
of coupling comes from interaction constraints: temporal-task satisfaction alone
does not ensure collision avoidance, communication maintenance, or
task-required relative-motion relations. Classical multi-robot stabilization,
collision avoidance, and connectivity maintenance therefore remain relevant even
when the high-level task is formal
\citep{dimarogonas2006feedback, zavlanos2011graph, ji2007distributed}. Work
combining temporal objectives with relative-motion, connectivity, or
communication-free coordination
\citep{guo2014controlling, guo2014cooperative, guo2015hybrid,
guo2015communicationfree} shows that task satisfaction and team integrity
cannot be separated cleanly, while graph-based local interaction models and
embedded graph grammars
\citep{mcnew2006locally, smith2009multi, pickem2013complete, guo2016graphgrammar}
offer compact ways to encode neighbor-dependent behaviors and structural
reconfiguration. The persistent tension is that global synthesis captures
coupling explicitly but scales poorly, whereas local synthesis scales better but
must maintain distributed consistency during execution. This also explains why
purely symbolic and purely geometric views are both incomplete: a symbolic plan
may visit the required regions and perform the required actions but fail to
maintain safe separation or communication, while a geometrically safe motion may
not preserve the required temporal ordering or collaboration semantics. The
coordination problem therefore lies at the interface between symbolic progress
and continuous team integrity, a theme developed in the case studies of
Chapters~\ref{chap:bottom-up}--\ref{chap:communication}. It also clarifies the
organization of the technical chapters: Chapter~\ref{chap:bottom-up} follows a
bottom-up view, where robots begin with local temporal tasks and coordinate when
plans require repair, assistance, or consistency with other agents, whereas
Chapter~\ref{chap:top-down} follows a top-down view, where a global mission is
decomposed into partially ordered subtasks and assigned to suitable subteams.
The former captures asynchronous local operation and execution-time dependence,
while the latter captures global mission structure and team-level temporal
requirements.

\subsection{Online coordination under communication constraints and human supervision}
\label{sec:ch01:rw:online}

The coordination models reviewed above still assume, to different degrees, that the
relevant task structure can be determined before execution and that enough information is
available to keep the team consistent. Practical deployment is more fluid. New requests
may arise after the mission begins, temporary collaborations may be required only at
specific states, communication may be intermittent, and human operators may need to
validate or redirect decisions without controlling every motion. Coordination then becomes
a problem of managing evolving commitments and delayed information while execution
continues.
The literature on online coordination therefore exposes a central asymmetry. A robot can
often detect a local need for help, repair, or communication before the rest of the team
has enough information to reason about it globally. The coordination architecture must
then mediate between local urgency and global consistency. This is the setting in which
temporary collaboration, bounded-delay information fusion, and topology-aware request
handling become more than implementation details; they determine whether the temporal
mission can still be interpreted coherently during execution.

 (I) \emph{Online and contingent collaboration}. Online and contingent
collaboration addresses the first part of this shift. In reactive task allocation,
proactive allocation, and hierarchical coordination under continual task streams, the task
set changes during execution and the team must update decisions without losing mission
coherence \citep{chen2025realtime, street2024rightplace, luo2025hulk}. Contingent
collaboration is more specific: a robot may need assistance only after reaching a
particular local state, after a resource becomes unavailable, or after a formation or
service condition is triggered. Classical reactive synthesis against a predefined
environment model \citep{kress2009temporal} does not fully capture this case, because
important coordination events are generated by the evolving internal state of the team
itself. Event-based collaborative mechanisms with service and formation requests
therefore treat requests as coordination objects in~\citet{guo2016graphgrammar}, rather than
external disturbances. The team must decide not only how to respond to change, but also
when a request should be generated, how it should propagate, and how the surrounding local
plans should absorb it.

 (II) \emph{Coordination under intermittent or limited communication}.
Communication constraints make online coordination harder. In subterranean exploration,
disaster response, large indoor facilities, and infrastructure inspection, communication
may be limited by range, occlusion, or network instability. Intermittent communication
protocols allow robots to operate independently and then re-establish contact through
planned meetings or rendezvous \citep{stump2011connectivity, hollinger2013sampling,
nikou2016cooperative}. This restores autonomy between communication events, but it also
means that plans, maps, and dependency information may be stale. Collaborative exploration
makes the same point. Frontier-based and information-gain methods benefit from shared maps
and timely mutual awareness \citep{yamauchi1997frontier, burgard2000collaborative,
sheng2006distributed}; once that assumption is weakened, the team must also decide when to
reconnect, whether to deploy relays, and how to balance map growth against information
latency \citep{mosteo2013framework, dehoog2010role}. Recent studies connect these
communication constraints to richer temporal mission models \citep{guo2024teamwise,
guo2025moroco, ansari2024colossi, silva2026probabilistic}. The later chapters adopt the
stronger view that communication maintenance is part of the planning problem, not a
passive execution condition.
These communication-aware problems differ from ordinary online replanning in an important
respect. If every robot has immediate access to the same information, then replanning can
be viewed mainly as a computational problem. Under intermittent communication, however,
the location and timing of information exchange become part of the feasible behavior. A
plan may be locally executable but globally unsafe if delayed information prevents another
robot or the operator from updating a dependent commitment in time. This observation is
the main reason that Chapter~\ref{chap:communication} treats communication topology,
exploration progress, and temporal request handling as a single coordination problem.


 (III) \emph{Human-fleet interaction and mixed-initiative supervision}.
Human-fleet interaction introduces partial information and selective authority:
operators are often better suited to specify goals, resolve ambiguity, approve
risky behavior, or intervene when priorities change, while the fleet should
still handle local planning and execution autonomously. Direct teleoperation
scales poorly with fleet size and becomes less practical under delayed or
intermittent communication. This has motivated work on human--swarm and
human--multi-robot interaction, where scalable supervision depends on
abstraction, situational awareness, and control modes that let operators
influence the mission without micromanaging individual robots
\citep{crandall2017human, nam2018survey, dahiya2023survey,
lyons2025examining}. Mixed-initiative and scaled-autonomy approaches address
this issue by shifting decision authority according to uncertainty, task
difficulty, and operator intent
\citep{jiang2019mixed, kortenkamp2016integrating}. In temporal robotic planning,
however, human intervention cannot be treated as an isolated override: it must
be incorporated into a coordination architecture that preserves task progress,
inter-robot consistency, and mission-level safety, especially when the operator
receives delayed or incomplete information about the fleet state
\citep{jhunjhunwala2021ihero, orton2020madcat, walker2023ar}. The useful
abstraction is therefore not a binary choice between autonomy and teleoperation,
but a coordination layer in which human input, robot-to-robot information flow,
and temporal task semantics remain mutually consistent.

\subsection{Uncertainty, abstraction and dynamic tasks}
\label{sec:ch01:rw:extensions}

Several cross-cutting developments connect the preceding lines of work. They do not form a
single unified school, but together they mark a clear change in emphasis: uncertainty,
abstraction, safety, and evolving task semantics are now part of the core planning problem
rather than secondary complications.

 (I) \emph{Probabilistic temporal-task planning}. Markov decision processes,
probabilistic model checking, and multi-objective verification make it possible to
optimize satisfaction probability, cost, or risk under stochastic dynamics
\citep{ding2011mdp, wolff2012robust, forejt2011quantitative, forejt2012pareto,
kwiatkowska2011prism}. Learning-based variants combine temporal specifications with
reinforcement learning when the transition model is not fully known
\citep{hasanbeig2019reinforcement, bozkurt2020control, wang2015temporal}. These approaches
broaden the reach of formal planning, but they do not remove the need for temporal
reasoning; they relocate it into rewards, policies, or probabilistic verification
objectives. Recent work also extends this direction to communication-constrained and
partially known multi-robot settings \citep{silva2026probabilistic, zhao2025noregret},
where the uncertainty is not only stochastic but also shaped by information flow.

 (II) \emph{Safety requirements and constrained satisfaction}. A second
development is a more structured treatment of safety. Safety can be encoded as a temporal
invariant, but long-horizon operation under uncertainty also requires notions of
recoverability, resilience, and constrained satisfaction. Minimum-violation planning
treats some rules as costly but violable \citep{tumova2013least, tumova2013minimum,
vasile2017minimum}, while safe exploration and stochastic-hybrid synthesis emphasize the
ability to avoid or recover from unsafe behavior under uncertain dynamics
\citep{moldovan2012safe, turchetta2016safe, laurenti2020formal}. From this perspective,
safety is not merely a propositional side condition. It affects how plans are compared,
revised, relaxed, and executed when exact task satisfaction is no longer guaranteed. This
view is particularly important in multi-robot settings, where safety and feasibility may
also depend on team composition, communication structure, and local collaboration
constraints.

 (III) \emph{Hierarchical and symbolic abstraction}. A third development is
abstraction. The cost of formal synthesis depends heavily on the finite model presented to
the planner. Symbolic abstraction, approximate refinement, and hierarchical synthesis seek
finite representations that preserve the distinctions relevant to the task while
suppressing irrelevant state detail \citep{tabuada2009verification,
haesaert2018verification, meyer2019hierarchical, nilsson2014incremental}. Temporal
abstraction, including options and other temporally extended behaviors
\citep{sutton1999between}, reduces the effective planning horizon and lets the planner
reason over structured behaviors rather than primitive transitions. This monograph follows
the same principle: abstraction is not a preprocessing step alone, but a modeling decision
shaped by task structure, scalability, and online adaptation.

 (IV) \emph{Signal temporal logic and dynamic task models}. The fourth
development is the move from propositional temporal logic over static labels to richer and
more dynamic task models. Signal Temporal Logic is particularly important because its
semantics are defined over real-valued trajectories rather than only over abstract labels.
It can therefore express formation errors, relative distances, time-varying predicates,
and continuous safety margins. Multi-robot and optimization-based STL planning methods
\citep{lindemann2019coupled, liu2022stlq, kurtz2022mixed, sun2022multiagent,
sewlia2025maps2, yang2024stlmpc, buyukkocak2024sequential} bring temporal reasoning closer
to continuous control, although often at the cost of more difficult optimization. Dynamic
task models go further by allowing targets, propositions, semantic features, or
neighboring agents to change during execution \citep{zhang2024reactive,
kalluraya2023multirobot, aksaray2022online}. Taken together, these developments explain
why the later chapters combine formal task semantics with abstraction, adaptation,
communication-aware coordination, and richer models of uncertainty instead of treating
these ingredients separately.
Finally, dynamic task models sharpen the distinction between task updates and ordinary
disturbances. A disturbance changes the state of the system or the quality of a candidate
trajectory; a task update changes the condition under which the trajectory is judged. In
robot teams, such updates may be caused by moving targets, changing semantic labels, new
requests from the operator, or the appearance of uncontrollable neighboring agents. A
coordination method must therefore maintain both executable behavior and an up-to-date
interpretation of what the behavior is supposed to satisfy.


These four developments also mark a change in how formal task planning should be
interpreted. The classical abstraction-to-automaton pipeline remains a useful foundation,
but it is no longer sufficient as a complete deployment model. The planner must decide
what information to retain, which constraints are hard or soft, how to compare partial
satisfaction with strict satisfaction, and how to update the task representation when the
environment or mission changes. The methodological position of this monograph is that
these questions should be addressed inside the coordination architecture rather than
postponed to an implementation layer after synthesis has already been completed.
