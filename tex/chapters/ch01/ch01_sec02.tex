\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
This section includes a short introduction to the research background of the thesis, including the motion and task planning problem of mobile robots, the model-checking algorithm for verification and synthesis, and the control and coordination problem of multi-robot systems.
Then we provide a review of some recent work related to the remaining chapters of this work.



\subsubsection{Model-checking-based control synthesis}

The model-checking algorithms have been used for both control and plan synthesis of dynamic systems. As the first step, various strategies can be found in~\cite{alur2000discrete, girard2007approximately} to construct finite symbolic abstraction of the dynamic system.
An automated framework for controller synthesis is proposed in~\cite{tabuada2006linear} for discrete-time linear systems where the control objective is specified by LTL formulas over the output trajectory, which is more complex than the traditional goal of system stabilization and output regulation.
Under a similar setup,~\cite{aydin2013temporal} adds a quadratic function associated with the cost of satisfying the LTL control objective, which is ought to be minimized.
Discrete-time piecewise affine systems are considered in~\cite{yordanov2010formal, kloetzer2008fully, yordanov2012temporal}, where the control task is specified as LTL formulas over linear predicates of the statespace.
Moreover, dynamical systems modeled as Markov Decision Processes (MDP) are considered in~\cite{ding2011mdp} where a dynamic control policy is generated automatically to satisfy the LTL task specification with maximal probability.
A robustness index is defined and measured for the satisfiability of continuous-time signals under metric temporal logic (MTL) specifications in~\cite{fainekos2009robustness}.
Then control objectives specified as MTL formulas are considered in~\cite{abbas2013probabilistic} where a generic control strategy is proposed.


On the other hand, \cite{fainekos2009temporal} firstly proposed the complete framework of automated controller synthesis for autonomous robots under LTL tasks. The robot's motion is abstracted by its dynamical transitions within a partition of the workspace, as a finite transition system. Then a high-level discrete plan as a sequence of regions to visit is synthesized by the model-checking algorithm, which is then implemented by the low-level continuous controller. Graphical tools are  developed in~\cite{srinivas2013graphical} for non-expert end-users to formulate the intended tasks freely, which are then translated to LTL formulas.
Robot manipulation tasks given as LTL formulas are addressed in~\cite{he2015towards} with a multi-layered structure of real-time planning and actuation.

\subsubsection{Partially-known workspace}

A critical assumption of the model-checking-based formalism for control synthesis  mentioned above is that the workspace needs to be perfectly known and correctly modeled by the finite transition system.
Then the discrete plan is normally generated off-line and is executed by the robot no matter what has changed in the workspace. As also mentioned in~\cite{ding2011mdp}, the robot does not react to its actual observations, yielding that this formalism lacks of reconfigurability and real-time adaptation.

Some existing work takes into account the case when a complete representation of the workspace is not available. In~\cite{ding2011mdp, wolff2012robust}, the robot's motion within the uncertain workspace is modeled as nondeterministic Markov decision processes (MDP), where the LTL task specification is satisfied by the proposed control strategy with maximal probability. Instead, a two-player General Reactivity GR(1) game between the robot and the environment is constructed in~\cite{kress2009temporal, wongpiromsarn2010receding} and a receding-horizon control and planning scheme is introduced. Then a winning strategy  can be synthesized by exhaustively searching through all allowed combinations of the robot movements and the admissible workspaces.

Instead of aiming for an off-line plan that covers every  situation, we propose in our work~\cite{guo2013revising}, which can be found in Chapter~\ref{chapter:partially}, to create firstly a preliminary plan based on the  initially-available knowledge about the robot dynamics and the workspace model. Then while executing this plan, the robot gathers real-time observations about the workspace and feedback for the plan execution status, based on which the plan can be verified and revised to ensure its correctness and feasibility. Similar ideas appear in~\cite{livingston2012backtracking} by locally patching the invalid transitions, which however can not handle changes of the regions' properties.
A hybrid motion planning algorithm is proposed in~\cite{maly2013iterative} that refines the workspace model iteratively during run time based on sensory updates, under task specifications given as co-safe LTL formulas.

\subsubsection{Infeasible task}
The initial knowledge of the workspace might be partially incorrect, which may render the intended task infeasible. As also mentioned in~\cite{fainekos2011revising, kim2012approximate, raman2011analyzing}, the nominal framework presented above simply reports a failure when the given task specification is not  {realizable}. It is desired that users could get feedbacks about why the planning has failed and  how to resolve this failure.  \cite{fainekos2011revising} and \cite{kim2012approximate} address this problem systematically by finding the relaxed specification automaton that is closest to the original one and can be fulfilled by the system.  \cite{raman2011analyzing} introduces a way to analyze the environment and system components contained in the infeasible specification, and identify the possible cause. The main difference between our work~\cite{guo2012infeasible} and the above references  is that we put emphasis on how to synthesize the motion plan that fulfills the infeasible task the most, instead of analyzing its infeasible parts.

The problem of synthesizing a least-violating control strategy under a set of safety rules are addressed in~\cite{tumova2013least, tumova2013minimum}. However the level of satisfiability is measured differently from our approach in~\cite{guo2012infeasible, guo2015multi}, as discussed in~Chapter~\ref{chapter:partially}. In particular, we not only measure {how many} states along the plan violate the safety specifications, but also {how much} each of those states violates these specifications.
\cite{lahijanian2015time} proposes a similar solution to~\cite{guo2012infeasible} but an additional weighting is enforced  to the set of atomic propositions.
Moreover, we introduce in~\cite{guo2012infeasible, guo2015multi} the notion of soft and hard constraints as two distinctive parts of the task specification, where the hard constraints concern safety requirements and soft constraints are for system performances.


\subsubsection{Motion and action planning}
To specify robotic tasks of practical interest, it is often necessary to include various actions at different regions. Thus we propose in our work~\cite{guo2013motion} a generic framework that combines the  model-checking-based robot motion planning with action planning using action description languages (ADL), as described later in Chapter~\ref{chapter:action}. Some relevant work can be found that integrates the model-checking-based motion planning with action planning. In~\cite{smith2011optimal},  since the underlying actions can only be performed at fixed regions, the specification can be easily restated as regions to visit. Or independent propositions are created for each action in~\cite{kress2009temporal} since the actions can be activated and de-activated at any time. The above approaches are not be applicable if some actions can only be performed when the workspace, or the robot itself has to satisfy certain conditions, or choices have to be made among the allowed actions.


\subsubsection{Multi-robot system with temporal tasks}

The above motion and task planning framework has also been extended to multi-robot systems that consist of multiple dynamical robots.
Many existing work can be found on decomposing a global task specification to bisimilar local ones in a top-down manner, which can then be executed by the robots in a synchronized~\cite{kloetzer2010automatic} and partially-synchronized~\cite{ding2011automatic} manner. Uncertainties in traveling time are tackled in~\cite{ulusoy2012robust} where an optimal path planning algorithm is proposed for multi-robot systems under a global task specification. \cite{karaman2011sampling} formulates the multi-vehicle routing problem under a global LTL task as a mixed integer linear programming (MILP) problem and the derived local plans  need to be executed in a synchronized fashion by each robot.  There are several drawbacks of this top-down approach: the plans for the whole team are synthesized by the central unit, with a high computational complexity subjected to the combinatorial blowup; it is vulnerable to robot failures and contingencies during run time.

From an opposite viewpoint, we assume that the local task specifications are assigned locally to each robot and  there is no specified global task. Namely we consider a team of cooperative robots with different, independently-assigned, even conflicting individual tasks as considered in our work~\cite{guo2012infeasible, guo2014distributed, guo2015multi, guo2015bottom}, which can be found in Chapters~\ref{chapter:partially}-\ref{chapter:continuous}. This {loosely coupled} {bottom-up} formulation is particularly useful for multi-robot systems where the robots have heterogeneous capabilities and distinctive task assignment.
As explained in the remaining chapters, it allows more flexibility of the system since plans and decisions are made locally by each robot~\cite{guo2014distributed}; conflicts are resolved and collaborations are coordinated during run time~\cite{guo2015multi, guo2015bottom}. A similar formalism is adopted in~\cite{filippidis2012decentralized} where a  decentralized framework for collaborative feasibility verification is proposed, which however does not include the way to resolve potentially-conflicting tasks.
A receding-horizon algorithm for distributed coordination is proposed in~\cite{tumova2014receding} for multi-robot systems under dependent LTL task specifications.


\subsubsection{Relative-motion constraints}
Relative-motion constraints often arise in multi-robot systems as the robots coexist within the same workspace,  such as collision avoidance~\cite{dimarogonas2006feedback}, network connectivity~\cite{ji2007distributed,zavlanos2011graph}, or relative-velocity constraints~\cite{guo2014controlling}.
As also pointed out in~\cite{guo2014controlling, zavlanos2011graph, zavlanos2007flocking}, obeying these constraints is of great importance for the stability, safety and integrity of the overall multi-robot team, which however are often imposed by assumption rather than treated as extra control objectives. We address a version of this problem in our early work~\cite{guo2014cooperative}, where we propose a dynamic leader-follower coordination and control strategy such that the relative-distance constraints among neighboring robots are respected. Later in our work~\cite{guo2015hybrid, guo2015communication} as included in Chapter~\ref{chapter:continuous}, we improve this scheme by  a fully decentralized and communication-free solution that is applicable to low-cost robotic systems equipped with only range and angle sensors, but without communication capabilities.
Different from~\cite{filippidis2012decentralized, guo2014cooperative} where a satisfying discrete plan is enough, the initial plan synthesis algorithm proposed in~\cite{guo2015hybrid, guo2015communication} minimizes the bottle-neck cost of a satisfying plan. The proposed motion control scheme guarantees almost global convergence and the satisfaction of relative-distance constraints at all time, for an {arbitrary} number of leaders that are active under different local goals. Most related literature only allows for a single leader~\cite{guo2014cooperative, ren2005survey} or multiple leaders with the same global goal~\cite{ogren2001control, zavlanos2007flocking}.
Additionally, three different local coordination policies are proposed in~\cite{guo2015communication} to incorporate different types of local tasks. Different from~\cite{kloetzer2011multi}, these policies are locally applicable to robots without explicit communication modules.

\subsubsection{Control with Embedded Graph Grammars}
As mentioned above, relative-motion constrains are crucial for the safety and stability of multi-robot systems in general. However, most of related work neglects this aspect, e.g, inter-robot collision is not handled formally in~\cite{guo2015multi,tumova2014receding} and connectivity of the communication network is taken for granted in most cases~\cite{guo2015bottom, ulusoy2013optimality}.
We take advantage of Embedded Graph Grammars (EGGs) in our work~\cite{guo2016hybrid} as described later in Chapter~\ref{chapter:continuous}, to specify local interaction and communication rules among the robots, in order to address simultaneously local LTL tasks and relative-motion constraints.
EGGs have been  introduced in~\cite{mcnew2006locally, mcnew2007solving}.
They provide a natural framework to encode the robot dynamics, local information exchange and switching control modes in a unified manner.
Their successful applications to multi-robot systems can be found in, e.g., coverage control~\cite{mcnew2007solving}, self-reconfiguration of modular robots~\cite{pickem2013complete}, and autonomous multi-robot deployment~\cite{smith2009multi}.
Note that only local interactions or communication are needed for the execution of EGGs, yielding it suitable for large-scale multi-robot systems.



\subsubsection{Contingent service and formation tasks}

%In~\cite{guo2015contingent}, we focus on the scenario where the robots within the multi-robot system are allowed to request and exchange contingent tasks with each other during run time.

Two common cooperative robotic tasks, namely service and formation tasks, are taken into account in our work~\cite{guo2015contingent}, of which the details are given in Chapter~\ref{chapter:contingent}.
Service requests are of particular interest  as they encapsulate the scenario where one robot needs another robot's assistance on a short-term task. The service requests can only be exchanged among the robots in real-time, which means that each robot has to react to the received service requests in a contingent way.
Similar work on real-time reactivity for dynamical systems under temporal tasks can be found in~\cite{kress2007s}, where sensory inputs are included in the General Reactivity GR(1) formulas to take into account possibly dynamical environments, as well as in~\cite{kress2009temporal}, where similar techniques are applied for various single-robot and multi-robot applications.
The local plans of each robot are synthesized off-line to handle all modeled changes in the environment, which is however not feasible in our formulation as the service and formation requests are exchanged in real-time under a non-predefined manner.

Another common cooperative robotic  task arises  when an robot requests from another one to form a relative configuration  to accomplish a collaborative task. We denote such a requirement in~\cite{guo2015contingent} as a formation request.
Similar ideas of imposing formation constraints for multi-robot systems appeared in~\cite{loizou2004automatic}, where however a global formation task for the whole team is embodied.
In the same vein, although the formation control has been extensively studied for multi-robot systems, e.g., in~\cite{ji2007distributed, ogren2001control, olfati2006flocking}, we enforce prescribed performance constraints on the transient response of the formation process in~\cite{guo2015contingent}.
The prescribed performance control problem was originally studied for high-order MIMO nonlinear single-robot systems~\cite{bechlioulis2008robust} and was recently extended to multi-robot systems with single integrator dynamics~\cite{karayiannidis2012multi} and nonlinear dynamics~\cite{bechlioulis2014robust}. However, the existing prescribed performance control technique is enhanced in our work~\cite{guo2015contingent} by: (i) considering transient performance specifications on the overall formation error, in contrast to the relative coordinates approach in~\cite{bechlioulis2014robust}, thus simplifying the control design; (ii) combining it with the high-level discrete planning such that various control modes are activated according to the real-time  execution of the discrete plan.
