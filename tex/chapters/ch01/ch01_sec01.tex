\section{Background}
\label{sec:ch01_background}

\subsection{Smart robotic systems in complex environments}
\label{sec:ch01:background:systems}


Advances in sensing, embedded computation, communication, and control have
accelerated the deployment of smart robotic systems in both everyday and
mission-critical environments. In many modern applications, robots no longer
operate as isolated machines that execute fixed routines in closed settings~\cite{atzori2010internet}.
Instead, they function as mobile components of larger networked systems, where
information is exchanged among robots, sensing devices, and supervisory
infrastructure. This broader trend has been reinforced by the development of
connected physical systems, in which perception, decision making, and actuation
are increasingly coupled through communication and shared data
streams.
Within this broader landscape, multi-robot systems have become an important
class of autonomous systems. A team of robots can distribute mobility, sensing,
and manipulation capabilities over a large workspace, while preserving the
ability to act locally when conditions change~\cite{arai2002advances,parker2008multiple}.
Such systems are not merely
scaled versions of single-robot platforms. Their collective behavior depends on
how local decisions interact through shared space, shared resources,
communication constraints, and task dependencies. Consequently, the system-level
question is not only how an individual robot moves or acts, but how multiple
robots coordinate their decisions in a coherent and reliable
way~\cite{arai2002advances,parker2008multiple,torreno2017cooperative}.


The need for coordinated autonomy is evident in several representative
application domains. In service settings, mobile robots are increasingly used
for delivery, inspection, cleaning, assistance, and other user-facing functions
in dynamic environments shared with humans and infrastructure~\cite{lee2021service}.
These systems
must operate persistently, respond to changing requests, and maintain safe and
predictable behavior despite environmental variation and incomplete
information.
A similarly strong motivation arises in search-and-rescue and disaster-response
operations. In hazardous environments, robot teams can extend sensing reach,
reduce human exposure to danger, and maintain activity in areas that are
difficult or unsafe for direct human access~\cite{murphy2014disaster}. Such scenarios often involve
uncertain terrain, incomplete maps, intermittent communication, and rapidly
changing operational priorities. They therefore provide a natural setting for
coordination methods that must combine autonomy, robustness, and online
adaptation~\cite{macaluso2020multi}.
Related coordination demands also appear in logistics, field robotics,
infrastructure inspection, and environmental monitoring. Although these domains
differ in their physical details, they share a common systems feature~\cite{parker2008multiple}.
Multiple robots must carry out spatially distributed activities under coupled
constraints, while preserving mission-level correctness and operational
efficiency~\cite{torreno2017cooperative}.

\subsection{Coordination requirements in multi-robot systems}
\label{sec:ch01:background:requirements}


Multi-robot systems are attractive because many large-scale tasks are naturally
parallel, geographically distributed, and heterogeneous. A team can provide
wider coverage than a single robot, reduce completion time through concurrent
execution, and improve resilience through redundancy~\cite{arai2002advances,
parker2008multiple}. Heterogeneous teams offer
an additional advantage, since sensing, mobility, communication, and
manipulation capabilities need not be concentrated on one platform. Different
robots can specialize and cooperate so that the team accomplishes tasks beyond
the capability of any individual member~\cite{fioretto2018distributed}.
These advantages, however, materialize only when coordination is handled
carefully. If robots compete for the same workspace region, obstruct one
another, or request assistance without a consistent protocol, the benefits of
parallelism can be quickly offset by conflict, delay, or deadlock. The same
team that improves performance under proper coordination may behave
inefficiently or unsafely when decisions are generated independently without
accounting for inter-robot coupling. Coordination is therefore not a secondary
layer added after planning. It is a central part of the planning problem
itself~\cite{parker2008multiple,torreno2017cooperative}.


The coordination problem becomes especially demanding when missions are
specified at a high level. At the continuous level, each robot must move under
geometric, kinematic, and dynamic constraints while avoiding unsafe regions and
other moving agents~\cite{lavalle2006planning}. At the discrete level, robots
must choose actions, respect preconditions, and reason about how local choices
affect future task feasibility~\cite{ghallab2004automated}. In realistic
missions, these two layers are tightly coupled. A robot may need to reach a
region before activating a service behavior, delay an action until another
robot has completed a prerequisite step, or revisit locations repeatedly while
maintaining safety and resource consistency.
This coupling is difficult to capture with simple goal-reaching formulations.
Many tasks require ordering, persistence, surveillance, response, and exclusion
properties that extend over long horizons. Temporal logics and automata-based
methods provide a principled language for such requirements, because they can
encode safety, reachability, recurrence, and precedence within a single formal
framework~\cite{baier2008principles,belta2007symbolic,tabuada2006linear}. Once
multiple robots are involved, these requirements often become relational. One
robot may depend on another for synchronized assistance, resource handoff,
temporary formation, or conflict-free access to a shared workspace. Even when
each robot is assigned a local task, the resulting motion and action plans are
rarely independent.

\subsection{Open challenges and methodological gap}
\label{sec:ch01:background:gap}


The challenge described in
Section~\ref{sec:ch01:background:requirements} becomes more severe when the
environment or task information is incomplete. In many deployments, a robot
does not begin with a perfect semantic model of the workspace. Traversability,
obstacles, communication quality, and region properties may become known only
during execution. Moreover, the relevant task set may evolve online. New
service requests can arise from operators, users, or peer robots, and
previously feasible plans may become suboptimal or invalid after new
information is acquired.
These effects are especially pronounced in long-horizon and collaborative
missions. A robot may need to interrupt its nominal plan to assist another team
member, restore communication opportunities, satisfy a contingent request, or
react to a revised mission priority. Such events are difficult to handle within
a purely offline pipeline. Replanning from scratch after every change is often
computationally expensive, while purely reactive local rules may fail to
preserve task-level correctness. The central difficulty is therefore not only
how to synthesize an initial plan, but how to maintain coordinated task
satisfaction as knowledge, requests, and interaction patterns evolve over time.


Existing coordination paradigms have made substantial progress, but they often
address only part of the full problem. Centralized approaches can optimize over
rich task specifications and explicitly encode coupling constraints, yet their
computational burden tends to grow rapidly with the number of robots, task
interactions, and synchronization requirements. This difficulty is especially
severe for temporally rich missions, where abstraction and automaton
construction already impose significant complexity before coordination is added
to the planning process.
At the opposite end, loosely coupled and distributed methods improve
scalability and resilience to local disturbances, but they often rely on
simplified task models, limited forms of inter-robot dependence, or weaker
correctness guarantees. Methods designed for static task sets do not extend
naturally to continual requests, partial knowledge, or dynamic communication
patterns. Similarly, methods that adapt well online do not always support
expressive task semantics or rigorous task-level guarantees.
A clear methodological gap therefore remains. There is a need for frameworks
that combine high-level temporal-task semantics with computationally manageable
coordination mechanisms, while also accommodating partial knowledge, online
task evolution, and contingent collaboration. This gap motivates the literature
review in Section~\ref{ch01:sec:related_work} and the technical development in the later chapters.
