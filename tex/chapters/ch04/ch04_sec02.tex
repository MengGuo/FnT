\section{Hierarchical Coordination of Large-Scale Fleets}\label{ch04:subsec:task}

Large-fleet temporal coordination is constrained both by logic, because missions
impose ordering, concurrency, and collaboration requirements, and by
combinatorics, because heterogeneous robots must be grouped and deployed under
capability and availability constraints. Monolithic products between temporal
automata and all robot models preserve correctness but scale poorly with robot
and task counts
\cite{kantaros2020stylus,schillinger2018simultaneous,luo2022temporal,
leahy2021scalable}, motivating hierarchical decompositions for large fleets
\cite{chen2025realtime,liu2024time,luo2025simultaneous,gosrich2025online,
luo2025hulk}. This section develops a three-layer architecture: temporal
decomposition with abstract team assignment, concrete team formation, and local
coordination with motion generation.

%==============================
\subsection{Temporal mission with uncertain subtasks}
\label{ch04:subsec:uncertain_mission}

In contrast to the previous section, the present setting considers a collection
of mission formulas whose collaborative tasks may have partially unknown
internal subtask structures. The formulation specifies the fleet, mission set,
and objective in a form suitable for hierarchical decomposition and team-level
coordination.
Consider a fleet of $N$ robots operating in a shared workspace
$\mathcal{W}\subset\mathbb{R}^{3}$, and let
$\mathcal{N}\triangleq\{1,\cdots,N\}$ be the robot index set. Each robot
$i\in\mathcal{N}$ is characterized by its current position
$x_i\in\mathcal{W}$, a reference velocity $v_i\in\mathbb{R}^{3}$, and a set of
primitive actions $\mathcal{A}_i$. The local plan of robot $i$ is represented
by a timed sequence:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:tau}
\tau_i \triangleq
(t_i^1,p_i^1,a_i^1)(t_i^2,p_i^2,a_i^2)\cdots,
\end{equation}
where $t_i^\ell\geq 0$ is the execution time, $p_i^\ell\in\mathcal{W}$ is the
target location, and $a_i^\ell\in\mathcal{A}_i$ is the action to be executed.
Thus, robot $i$ moves to $p_i^\ell$ and initiates action $a_i^\ell$ at time
$t_i^\ell$ for each $\ell\geq 1$.

Let $\Phi \triangleq \{\varphi_1,\cdots,\varphi_M\}$ be a finite set of
missions. Each mission $\varphi_m\in\Phi$ is an sc-LTL formula over a
collaborative-task alphabet
$\boldsymbol{\omega}_m \triangleq
\{\omega_{m,1},\cdots,\omega_{m,K_m}\}$, namely
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:mission_formula}
\varphi_m \in \mathrm{sc\mbox{-}LTL}(\boldsymbol{\omega}_m),
\end{equation}
where the syntax and semantics follow
Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries}. Each collaborative task
$\omega_{m,k}\in\boldsymbol{\omega}_m$ is characterized by an execution region
$S_{m,k}\subset\mathcal{W}$, a duration estimator
$\eta_{m,k}(\cdot)$, and a collection of required subtasks defined as:
\[
\Xi_{m,k}\triangleq
\{(n_{m,k}^{j},a_{m,k}^{j},s_{m,k}^{j})\}_{j=1}^{J_{m,k}},
\]
where each tuple $(n_{m,k}^{j},a_{m,k}^{j},s_{m,k}^{j})$ specifies that at
least $n_{m,k}^{j}$ robots are required to perform action
$a_{m,k}^{j}$ at location $s_{m,k}^{j}$. Accordingly,
$\omega_{m,k}$ is written as
$\omega_{m,k}\triangleq(S_{m,k},\eta_{m,k},\Xi_{m,k})$.
The uncertainty lies in the internal subtask structure: the number of subtasks,
their precise locations, or their effective durations may be unspecified when
the mission formula is issued, unlike models with fixed deterministic task
alphabets~\cite{sahin2019multirobot}. The estimator
$\eta_{m,k}(n,a,\mathcal{N}')$ depends on the assigned subset and captures
composition-dependent duration and saturation effects
\cite{ferreira2021survey,krizmancic2022cooperative_ag,apt2009generic,liu2024time}.

Let $t_0$ denote the initial planning time, and let $t_m^{\mathrm{f}}$ denote
the first time at which mission $\varphi_m$ is satisfied. The completion time
of mission $\varphi_m$ is defined by
$T_m \triangleq t_m^{\mathrm{f}}-t_0$.
Thus, the fleet objective is to synthesize collective plans
$\{\tau_i\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}}$ that satisfy all missions in $\Phi$ while
minimizing the average mission completion time, i.e.,
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:mission_set_objective}
\min_{\{\tau_i\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}}}
\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} T_m.
\end{equation}

\begin{problem}
\label{ch04:prob:uncertain_mission_set}
Given the fleet model in \eqref{ch04:eq:tau} and the mission set
$\Phi=\{\varphi_1,\cdots,\varphi_M\}$ with mission formulas in
\eqref{ch04:eq:mission_formula}, synthesize collective plans
$\{\tau_i\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}}$ such that all missions in $\Phi$ are satisfied
and the average mission completion time in
\eqref{ch04:eq:mission_set_objective} is minimized.
\end{problem}

This formulation combines temporal missions, uncertain collaborative subtasks,
and fleet-level execution constraints. Direct centralized formulations become
rapidly intractable as the numbers of missions, tasks, and robots increase,
which motivates the hierarchical treatment below.

\subsection{Simultaneous task decomposition and team assignment}
\label{ch04:subsubsec:simultaneous_assignment}

For the finite mission set
$\Phi \triangleq \{\varphi_1,\cdots,\varphi_M\}$, the next step is to decompose
missions into executable symbols while assigning them to abstract teams.
Synchronized products between mission automata and full fleet models preserve
correctness but scale poorly
\cite{schillinger2018simultaneous,luo2022temporal}. Existing scalability
mechanisms include local coordination~\cite{lindemann2019coupled}, decision
trees~\cite{chen2025realtime}, distributed sampling~\cite{kantaros2020stylus},
hierarchical decoupling~\cite{luo2025simultaneous}, and partial-order reasoning
\cite{liu2024time}. Here, decomposition and team assignment are performed
simultaneously within one automaton-guided search.
For each mission $\varphi_m \in \Phi$, let
\[
\mathcal{B}_{\varphi_m} \triangleq
(Q^m,Q_0^m,\Sigma^m,\delta^m,Q_F^m)
\]
be the B\"uchi automaton associated with $\varphi_m$, defined as in
Definition~\ref{ch02:def:NBA}. Let
$\mathcal{M} \triangleq \{1,\cdots,M\}$ be the mission index set. The fleet is
partitioned into $K$ abstract teams indexed by
$\mathcal{K} \triangleq \{1,\cdots,K\}$, where the value of $K$ is not fixed in
advance. To avoid conflict with the collaborative behaviors $C_k$ introduced in
Section~\ref{ch04:sec:po_decomp_assignment}, the teams are denoted here by
$\mathcal{T}_k$. Each team is described by an abstract capability profile to be
instantiated later, rather than by a fixed set of robot identities. Let
$\Gamma_k$ denote the local plan of team $\mathcal{T}_k$, written as a sequence
of task symbols assigned to that team.

\begin{problem}
\label{ch04:prob:simultaneous_assignment}
Given the mission set $\Phi$, determine the number of teams $K$, the capability
profiles of the teams $\{\mathcal{T}_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$, and the local
plans $\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$ such that all mission specifications are
satisfied under the temporal and fleet-capacity constraints, and the mission
execution cost is \emph{minimized}.
\end{problem}

The search is organized as a rooted tree
$\mathfrak{T} \triangleq (\mathcal{V},\rightarrow)$,
where $\mathcal{V}$ is the node set and
$\rightarrow \subseteq \mathcal{V}\times\mathcal{V}$ is the edge relation.
Each node
$\nu \triangleq
\bigl(\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}},
\{\widehat{Q}_m\}_{m\in\mathcal{M}}\bigr)$
contains two components: the current local plans of all teams and the sets of
currently reachable automaton states for all missions. The root node is
$\nu_0 \triangleq
\bigl(\emptyset,\{\widehat{Q}_m^0\}_{m\in\mathcal{M}}\bigr)$,
where $\widehat{Q}_m^0 \triangleq Q_0^m$. Algorithm~\ref{ch04:alg:ss-TaskAssign}
contains four stages: selection, expansion, bounding, and termination.


\emph{(I) Selection.}
At each iteration, a set of $P>0$ candidate nodes is selected for parallel
expansion. Denote this set by
$\mathcal{V}_P \triangleq \{\nu_1^\star,\cdots,\nu_P^\star\}$, where the nodes
are chosen from the current frontier in ascending order of a value function
$\chi:\mathcal{V}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$. This value function is defined
by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:search_value}
\chi(\nu) \triangleq
\max_{k\in\mathcal{K}} T_k
+ \eta_1 \sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}} \kappa_k
+ \eta_2 \sum_{m\in\mathcal{M}}
\min_{q_m\in\widehat{Q}_m}\psi_m(q_m,Q_F^m),
\end{equation}
where $\eta_1,\eta_2>0$ are weighting parameters, $T_k$ is the ending time of
the local plan $\Gamma_k$, $\kappa_k$ is the estimated cost of $\Gamma_k$, and
$\psi_m(q_m,Q_F^m)$ is the length of the shortest path from state $q_m$ to the
accepting set $Q_F^m$ in $\mathcal{B}_{\varphi_m}$. The first term in
\eqref{ch04:eq:search_value} estimates the current makespan, the second
penalizes costly assignments, and the third favors nodes with greater mission
progress. The weights $\eta_1$ and $\eta_2$ regularize the search order and
mainly affect the convergence rate rather than feasibility.
For each team $\mathcal{T}_k$, the local plan $\Gamma_k$ induces an abstract
capacity profile below:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:capacity-team}
\Lambda_k \triangleq
\{(\beta_k^j,a^j)\mid a^j\in\mathcal{A},\ \beta_k^j>0\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A} \triangleq \bigcup_{i\in\mathcal{N}}\mathcal{A}_i$ is the
global action set, and $\beta_k^j$ is the minimum number of robots capable of
action $a^j$ required by the tasks currently assigned to $\Gamma_k$. A node is
declared infeasible if the induced team requirements exceed the fleet
capability, that is, if
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:capacity-bound}
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}} \beta_k^j
\leq
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}} \mathds{1}(a^j\in\mathcal{A}_i),
\qquad \forall a^j\in\mathcal{A},
\end{equation}
is violated,
where the left-hand side is the required capacity and the right-hand side is
the available fleet capacity.

\emph{(II) Expansion.}
Each selected node $\nu_h^\star\in\mathcal{V}_P$ is expanded by assigning one
additional task symbol to one team. The candidate task set is given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:task-candidate}
\Omega^-_{\nu_h^\star} \triangleq
\bigcup_{m\in\mathcal{M}}
\Bigl\{
\omega\in\Sigma^m \,\Big|\, \exists q_m\in\widehat{Q}_m :
\delta^m(q_m,\omega)\neq\emptyset
\Bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $\delta^m:Q^m\times\Sigma^m\to 2^{Q^m}$ is the transition function of
$\mathcal{B}_{\varphi_m}$. Therefore, a task symbol is eligible only if it
enables at least one valid automaton transition for some mission.
For each $\omega\in\Omega^-_{\nu_h^\star}$ and each team index
$k\in\mathcal{K}$, a child node is constructed as:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:expand}
\nu^+ \triangleq
\Bigl(
\{\Gamma_1,\cdots,\Gamma_k^+,\cdots,\Gamma_K\},
\{\widehat{Q}_m^+\}_{m\in\mathcal{M}}
\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma_k^+$ is obtained by appending $\omega$ to $\Gamma_k$, and
$\widehat{Q}_m^+ \triangleq
\bigcup_{q_m\in\widehat{Q}_m}\delta^m(q_m,\omega)$
is the updated set of reachable states for mission $\varphi_m$. The edge
$(\nu_h^\star,\nu^+)$ is then added to the search tree $\mathfrak{T}$. If
$\Omega^-_{\nu_h^\star}=\emptyset$, the node admits no further expansion.

A new team can also be created during expansion. In that case, the index set is
enlarged to $\mathcal{K}\cup\{K+1\}$, an empty local plan is introduced for the
new team, and the selected task symbol is assigned to it. In this way, team
formation and task assignment emerge jointly from the search rather than being
fixed in advance.
After assigning a task $\omega_k^\ell$ to team $k$, the capacity profile is
updated by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:update-constraint}
\beta_k^j \triangleq
\max\{n_r \mid \exists \omega\in\Gamma_k
\text{ such that } (n_r,a^j,s_r)\in\omega\},
\qquad \forall a^j\in\mathcal{A},
\end{equation}
where $\beta_k^j$ is set to zero if no task in $\Gamma_k$ requires action
$a^j$. The corresponding estimated ending time is updated by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:estimated_time}
t_{\mathrm{e}}(\omega_k^\ell) \triangleq
\max_{\omega_j\in\texttt{Pre}(\omega_k^\ell)}
\bigl\{ t_{\mathrm{e}}(\omega_j) \bigr\}
+ T_{\mathrm{nav}}(S_k^{\ell-1},S_k^\ell)
+ T_{\mathrm{exec}}(\omega_k^\ell),
\end{equation}
where $\texttt{Pre}(\omega_k^\ell)$ is the set of preceding tasks of
$\omega_k^\ell$ that have already been assigned, and
$T_{\mathrm{nav}}(S_k^{\ell-1},S_k^\ell)$ is the estimated navigation time
between the execution regions of two consecutive tasks.

\begin{algorithm}[t!]
  \caption{Simultaneous task decomposition and team assignment}
  \label{ch04:alg:ss-TaskAssign}
  \SetAlgoLined
  \KwIn{$\Phi=\{\varphi_1,\cdots,\varphi_M\}$, automata
        $\{\mathcal{B}_{\varphi_m}\}$, fleet $\mathcal{N}$.}
  \KwOut{Number of teams $K$, capability profiles $\{\Lambda_k\}$, and local
         plans $\{\Gamma_k\}$.}

  Initialize $\mathfrak{T}=(\mathcal{V},\rightarrow)$ with
  $\mathcal{V}=\{\nu_0\}$.\;

  \While{the search is not terminated}{
    \tcc{Selection}
    Select $\mathcal{V}_P=\{\nu_1^\star,\cdots,\nu_P^\star\}$ by the value
    function in~\eqref{ch04:eq:search_value}.\;
    Compute $\Lambda_k$ for all $k\in\mathcal{K}$ by
    \eqref{ch04:eq:capacity-team}.\;

    \tcc{Expansion}
    \ForEach{$\nu_h^\star \in \mathcal{V}_P$}{
      Obtain $\Omega^-_{\nu_h^\star}$ by~\eqref{ch04:eq:task-candidate}.\;
      \ForEach{$\omega \in \Omega^-_{\nu_h^\star}$}{
        \If{a new team is created}{
          Create team $K+1$ and set $\Gamma_{K+1}=\emptyset$.\;
          Update the team index set $\mathcal{K}$.\;
        }
        \ForEach{$k \in \mathcal{K}$}{
          Generate $\nu^+$ by~\eqref{ch04:eq:expand}.\;
          Update $\widehat{Q}_m^+$ for all $m\in\mathcal{M}$.\;
          Update $\Lambda_k$ by~\eqref{ch04:eq:update-constraint}.\;
          Update $t_{\mathrm{e}}(\omega_k^\ell)$ by
          \eqref{ch04:eq:estimated_time}.\;
          \lIf{\eqref{ch04:eq:capacity-bound} is violated}
             {mark $\nu^+$ infeasible}
          Add $(\nu_h^\star,\nu^+)$ to $\mathfrak{T}$.\;
        }
      }
    }

    \tcc{Bounding}
    \ForEach{$\nu \in \mathcal{V}$}{
      Compute $\zeta(\nu)$ by~\eqref{ch04:eq:profile}.\;
      Prune dominated nodes by~\eqref{ch04:eq:dominance}.\;
    }
    Update $\overline{\mathcal{V}}$ by~\eqref{ch04:eq:frontier}.\;
  }

  \tcc{Termination}
  Compute $\overline{\mathcal{V}}^\star$ by~\eqref{ch04:eq:complete-nodes}.\;
  Select $\nu_{K^\star}^\star$ by~\eqref{ch04:eq:termination}.\;
  \Return $K^\star$, $\{\Lambda_k\}$, and $\{\Gamma_k\}$.\;
\end{algorithm}

\emph{(III) Bounding.}
To reduce unnecessary exploration, each node is evaluated through a performance
profile that aggregates the current team schedules, team costs, and mission
progress:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:profile}
\zeta(\nu) \triangleq
\Bigl[
\{T_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}},
\{\kappa_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}},
\Bigl\{
\min_{q_m\in\widehat{Q}_m}\psi_m(q_m,Q_F^m)
\Bigr\}_{m\in\mathcal{M}}
\Bigr],
\end{equation}
where $T_k$ is the ending time of $\Gamma_k$, $\kappa_k$ is the estimated cost
of $\Gamma_k$, and the last term measures the minimum distance from the current
mission-progress set $\widehat{Q}_m$ to the accepting set $Q_F^m$. The profile
$\zeta(\nu)$ is therefore a nonnegative vector of dimension $(2K+M)$.
Given two nodes $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$, node $\nu_1$ is said to dominate node
$\nu_2$ if
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:dominance}
\zeta(\nu_1)\leq \zeta(\nu_2), \qquad
\zeta(\nu_1)\neq \zeta(\nu_2),
\end{equation}
where the inequality is interpreted element-wise. In other words, $\nu_1$ has
no larger team-end time or team cost, and no worse mission progress, with
strict improvement in at least one component. The set of frontier nodes is then
defined by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:frontier}
\overline{\mathcal{V}} \triangleq
\bigl\{
\nu\in\mathcal{V} \,\big|\,
\nexists\,\nu'\in\mathcal{V} \text{ such that } \nu' \text{ dominates } \nu
\bigr\},
\end{equation}
where only nodes in $\overline{\mathcal{V}}$ are retained as candidates for
further expansion. This pruning rule discards strictly inferior branches while
preserving all non-dominated search directions.

\emph{(IV) Termination.}
The stages above are repeated until the computational budget is exhausted or no
new frontier nodes are generated. Among the frontier nodes, the set of complete
assignments is given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:complete-nodes}
\overline{\mathcal{V}}^\star \triangleq
\bigl\{
\nu\in\overline{\mathcal{V}} \,\big|\,
\widehat{Q}_m\cap Q_F^m\neq\emptyset,\ \forall m\in\mathcal{M}
\bigr\},
\end{equation}
which means that every mission has reached an accepting state. The final node is
selected by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:termination}
\nu_{K^\star}^\star \triangleq
\texttt*{arg\,min}_{\nu\in\overline{\mathcal{V}}^\star}\chi(\nu),
\end{equation}
where $\chi(\nu)$ is the value function in
\eqref{ch04:eq:search_value}. The resulting capability profiles
$\{\Lambda_k\}$ and local plans $\{\Gamma_k\}$ define the abstract team
assignment.

\begin{example}
\label{ch04:example:task-decomp}
Suppose the mission automata
contain the task symbols
$\omega_1,\omega_2,\omega_3,\omega_4,\omega_5$. The optimal leaf node
$\nu_{K^\star}^\star$ assigns these five tasks to three teams, so that
$K^\star=3$. One feasible outcome is that the first team executes
$\omega_1$ followed by $\omega_3$, the second executes $\omega_2$ followed by
$\omega_4$, and the third executes $\omega_5$.
\end{example}

The construction avoids a monolithic product over all mission automata and robot
models, and lets the number of teams emerge from the search. Unlike the
poset-based methods in~\cite{liu2024time,luo2025hulk}, each node directly tracks
reachable-state sets $\widehat{Q}_m$ for all missions, so decomposition and
abstract assignment are handled in one search process. Correctness and
completeness for the fixed mission set are stated next.

\begin{theorem}
\label{ch04:thm:correctness}
Consider an instance of Problem~\ref{ch04:prob:simultaneous_assignment} with a
fixed finite mission set $\Phi$. Suppose that the search is allowed to terminate
on the finite tree and that feasible team plans exist. Then
Algorithm~\ref{ch04:alg:ss-TaskAssign} returns local plans
$\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$ such that:
\emph{(I)} all temporal constraints encoded by the automata
$\{\mathcal{B}_{\varphi_m}\}_{m\in\mathcal{M}}$ are satisfied;
\emph{(II)} all fleet-capacity constraints in
\eqref{ch04:eq:capacity-team}--\eqref{ch04:eq:capacity-bound} are respected;
\emph{(III)} among the complete frontier nodes, the returned node minimizes the
search value in \eqref{ch04:eq:search_value}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Temporal correctness follows from the update of the reachable-state sets
$\widehat{Q}_m$ along enabled automaton transitions. Every root-to-leaf path
ending at a node in $\overline{\mathcal{V}}^\star$ satisfies
\eqref{ch04:eq:complete-nodes}, so for each mission index $m\in\mathcal{M}$
the corresponding task sequence induces an accepting run of
$\mathcal{B}_{\varphi_m}$. Hence the local plans
$\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$ satisfy all temporal constraints encoded by
the mission automata.
Fleet-capacity feasibility follows from the expansion and pruning rules.
Whenever a task is added to a team plan, the induced capability profile is
updated by \eqref{ch04:eq:update-constraint}, and every node violating the fleet
bound \eqref{ch04:eq:capacity-bound} is marked infeasible and removed from
further consideration. Therefore, every node that remains on the frontier
satisfies the capacity constraints.
The search space is finite because the mission set $\Phi$, the automata
$\mathcal{B}_{\varphi_m}$, their state sets $Q^m$, the alphabets $\Sigma^m$,
and the admissible team-capability profiles are all finite. The frontier update
\eqref{ch04:eq:frontier} removes only dominated nodes and therefore preserves at
least one representative of each non-dominated feasible solution class. Hence,
if feasible plans exist, at least one complete frontier node is generated in
finite time. By \eqref{ch04:eq:termination}, the returned node is the minimizer
of the value function \eqref{ch04:eq:search_value} over
$\overline{\mathcal{V}}^\star$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Capacity-based and redundancy-aware team formation}
\label{ch04:subsubsec:team_formation}

Given the abstract assignment $\nu_{K^\star}^\star$, each abstract team must be
instantiated by concrete robots. The capability profiles specify only aggregate
action requirements, not robot identities, so this subsection determines
disjoint concrete teams while allowing controlled redundancy.

\begin{problem}
\label{ch04:prob:team_formation}
Given the abstract assignment $\nu_{K^\star}^\star$ and the fleet
$\mathcal{N}$, determine the team formation as
$\overline{\mathcal{N}} \triangleq
\{\mathcal{N}_1,\cdots,\mathcal{N}_{K^\star}\}$,
where $\mathcal{N}_k$ is the set of robots assigned to the abstract team
$\mathcal{T}_k$. Each team must satisfy its required capacities, and the team
memberships must be disjoint, that is,
$\mathcal{N}_{k_1}\cap\mathcal{N}_{k_2}=\emptyset$ for all $k_1\neq k_2$.
The objective is to minimize the overall response time of the assigned
missions.
\end{problem}

The team-formation problem is cast as a redundancy-aware mixed-integer linear
program with capacity constraints induced by $\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$
and a min-max response-time objective. Let $b_{ik}\in\{0,1\}$ be the binary
decision variable indicating whether robot
$i\in\mathcal{N}$ is assigned to team $\mathcal{T}_k$. The capacity profile
$\Lambda_k$ from \eqref{ch04:eq:capacity-team} specifies the minimum number
$\beta_k^j$ of robots capable of action $a^j$ required by team $k$. To allow
certain redundancy, introduce a margin $\alpha_j\geq 1$ for each action
$a^j\in\mathcal{A}$. Then the team-capacity bounds are written as:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:bound_form}
\beta_k^j
\leq
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}} b_{ik}\,\mathds{1}(a^j\in\mathcal{A}_i)
\leq
\alpha_j\beta_k^j,
\qquad \forall a^j\in\mathcal{A},\ \forall k\in\mathcal{K},
\end{equation}
where the left inequality enforces the minimum required capacity and the right
inequality limits the amount of redundancy admitted for that action. In
addition, concrete team memberships must remain disjoint, which is imposed by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:disjoint_membership}
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}} b_{ik}\leq 1,
\qquad \forall i\in\mathcal{N},
\end{equation}
where each robot can belong to at most one team.
The margin $\alpha_j$ accounts for workload uncertainty and possible robot
unavailability. Larger values improve robustness but reserve more robots,
whereas smaller values increase utilization. The response-time objective is
defined next. Let
$S_k^1$ be the region of the first task in the local plan $\Gamma_k$. For each
robot $i$ and team $k$, define the expected arrival time by
$t_{ik}\triangleq
\widehat{t}_i+T_{\mathrm{nav}}(\widehat{x}_i,S_k^1)$,
where $\widehat{t}_i$ is the time at which robot $i$ becomes available and
$\widehat{x}_i$ is its position at that time. Once the team members are fixed,
the execution cost of team $\mathcal{N}_k$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:team_cost}
J_k \triangleq
\max_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}\{t_{ik}\}
+\sum_{\omega\in\Gamma_k} T_{\mathrm{exec}}(\omega),
\end{equation}
where the first term is the synchronization delay of the slowest robot
in team $k$, and the second term is the total execution time of the
tasks in $\Gamma_k$. The overall objective is given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:formation_objective}
\min \max_{k\in\mathcal{K}} J_k,
\end{equation}
which minimizes the worst response time across all teams.

With standard max linearization,
\eqref{ch04:eq:bound_form}--\eqref{ch04:eq:formation_objective} is a mixed-
integer linear program with $\mathcal{O}(|\mathcal{N}|K^\star)$ binary variables
and $\mathcal{O}(|\mathcal{A}|K^\star)$ capacity constraints. Although NP-hard,
it remains smaller than direct robot-to-subtask assignment because the number of
abstract teams is limited by the hierarchy
\cite{sahin2019multirobot,kurtz2021more}. Once $\{b_{ik}\}$ is obtained, the
concrete team formation is recovered by
$\mathcal{N}_k \triangleq
\{i\in\mathcal{N}\mid b_{ik}=1\},
\forall k\in\mathcal{K}$.
The plan of each robot $i\in\mathcal{N}_k$ is then induced by the local plan
$\Gamma_k$ of its team, namely
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:robot-plan}
\tau_i^{\mathrm{tsk}} \triangleq
(S_k^1,\omega_k^1)(S_k^2,\omega_k^2)\cdots(S_k^{L_k},\omega_k^{L_k}),
\qquad \forall i\in\mathcal{N}_k,
\end{equation}
where $\omega_k^\ell$ is the $\ell$th task in $\Gamma_k$, $S_k^\ell$ is its
associated execution region, and $L_k$ is the number of tasks assigned to team
$k$. Thus, every robot receives a concrete task sequence that is consistent
with the team assignment and the mission-level decomposition.

\begin{example}
\label{ch04:example:formation}
Consider the team formation induced by the
optimal abstract assignment $\nu_{K^\star}^\star$. In that example, the team
$\mathcal{N}_1=\{2,4,6,7,9\}$ executes the sequence
$\omega_2\omega_3\omega_6\omega_8$, the team
$\mathcal{N}_2=\{3,8,13,14,15\}$ executes $\omega_1\omega_5$, and the team
$\mathcal{N}_{K^\star}=\{5,10,11,17,18,19\}$ executes
$\omega_9\omega_{11}\omega_{10}$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Local task and trajectory coordination within teams}
\label{ch04:subsec:local_coordination}

Given the concrete formation
$\overline{\mathcal{N}}=\{\mathcal{N}_1,\cdots,\mathcal{N}_{K^\star}\}$, each
robot inherits the team sequence in \eqref{ch04:eq:robot-plan}. For team
$k$, let $\Gamma_k=(\omega_k^1,\omega_k^2,\cdots,\omega_k^{L_k})$, where task
$\omega_k^\ell$ has region $S_k^\ell$ and required subtasks
$\Xi_k^\ell=\{(n_j,a_j,s_j)\}_{j=1}^{J_k^\ell}$. Execution still requires a
timed local plan and feasible trajectory for each robot. To distinguish this
object from the fleet-level plan in Subsection~\ref{ch04:subsec:uncertain_mission}, denote the
local execution plan of robot $i$ during $\omega_k^\ell$ by:
\[
\widetilde{\tau}_i^\ell \triangleq
(t_i^1,p_i^1,a_i^1)(t_i^2,p_i^2,a_i^2)\cdots,
\]
where $t_i^r$ is the execution time, $p_i^r$ is the target position, and
$a_i^r$ is the action executed at that stage. The collective local plan for
task $\omega_k^\ell$ is therefore
$\widetilde{\tau}_k^\ell \triangleq
\{\widetilde{\tau}_i^\ell \mid i\in\mathcal{N}_k\}$, and the corresponding
robot trajectories are denoted by $\{x_i^\ell\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}$.
Local task allocation and trajectory generation are coupled because subtask
execution time depends on both the assigned robot and its route to the target
location. The subsection treats three cases: static and known subtasks, static
and unknown subtasks, and dynamic and known subtasks.

\begin{problem}
\label{ch04:prob:local_coordination}
Given a task $\omega_k^\ell$ with region $S_k^\ell$, required subtask set
$\Xi_k^\ell$, and assigned team $\mathcal{N}_k$, determine the local execution
plans $\widetilde{\tau}_k^\ell$ and the trajectories
$\{x_i^\ell\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}$ such that the task is executed feasibly and
the local makespan is minimized.
\end{problem}

The three cases are determined by $\Xi_k^\ell$: known fixed subtasks require
coordinated assignment and trajectory planning; initially unknown fixed subtasks
require exploration and online insertion; known moving subtasks require continual
reassignment and trajectory updates.

\subsubsection{Static and known subtasks}
\label{ch04:sec:static-known}

In the static and known case, both $J_k^\ell$ and
$\{s_j\}_{j=1}^{J_k^\ell}$ are predetermined, as in delivery or inspection.
For holonomic robots, the problem becomes multi-vehicle routing without return:
visit sequences are chosen to minimize the slowest completion time, and
straight-line connections give the trajectories. For non-holonomic robots,
sequencing and motion feasibility cannot be separated.
Each subtask location must be augmented by a feasible orientation, and
transitions between subtasks must respect the robot dynamics. This leads to a
joint optimization over execution sequences and motion primitives. A convenient
formulation is
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:augmented-cost}
J_k^\ell \triangleq
\min_{\{\widetilde{\tau}_i^\ell,x_i^\ell\}}
\max_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}
\Biggl\{
\sum_{(u,v)\in \widetilde{\tau}_i^\ell}
\min_{\kappa\in\mathbb{K}(x_u,x_v)}
\texttt{Cost}(\kappa)
\Biggr\},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{K}(x_u,x_v)$ is the set of feasible motion primitives
connecting the states $x_u$ and $x_v$. The cost in
\eqref{ch04:eq:augmented-cost} therefore combines subtask sequencing and
dynamically feasible motion. Practical implementations can employ motion
primitives such as Dubins curves~\cite{ny2011dubins,vavna2015dubins}, together
with hybrid-A$^\star$ type search~\cite{erke2020improved}, to obtain the plans
$\widetilde{\tau}_k^\ell$ and trajectories
$\{x_i^\ell\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}$.

\begin{example}
\label{ch04:example:StaticKnown}
In one static and known
case, a total of $14$ delivery subtasks are assigned to $5$ robots with
different dynamic constraints. The resulting assignments and trajectories differ
substantially between the holonomic and non-holonomic settings.
\end{example}

\subsubsection{Static and unknown subtasks}
\label{ch04:sec:staticunknown}

In the static and unknown case, the subtasks in $\Xi_k^\ell$ are initially
unknown but fixed after discovery, as in search-and-rescue. The approach combines
collaborative exploration of $S_k^\ell$ with online insertion of newly revealed
subtasks. Exploration accounts for heterogeneous velocities, initial positions,
sensing footprints, and, in structured environments, obstacle avoidance through
frontier-based mechanisms~\cite{holz2010evaluating}. When a feasible new
subtask is discovered, the local plans are updated by solving:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:min-disruption}
\min_{\{\widetilde{\tau}_i^{\ell,+},x_i^{\ell,+}\}}
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}
\Bigl(
\texttt{Cost}(\widetilde{\tau}_i^{\ell,+})
-
\texttt{Cost}(\widetilde{\tau}_i^\ell)
\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{\tau}_i^{\ell,+}$ is the updated local plan obtained after
inserting the newly discovered subtasks. The objective in \eqref{ch04:eq:min-disruption} measures insertion cost;
online assignment selects the feasible robot with the smallest increment and
updates the corresponding trajectory. Non-holonomic robots use the same
motion-primitive construction as in the static and known case.

\begin{example}
\label{ch04:example:StaticUnknown}
In one static and
unknown case, five robots are assigned to a task containing $8$ initially
unknown targets. The initial trajectories are chosen for coverage, and the
plans are then updated as targets are discovered. The resulting execution
simultaneously explores the task region and completes all revealed subtasks.
\end{example}

\subsubsection{Dynamic and known subtasks}
\label{ch04:sec:dynamicknown}

In the dynamic and known case, $\Xi_k^\ell$ and the initial locations are known,
but the subtasks move during execution, as in cooperative pursuit and capture.
Static plans therefore become suboptimal or infeasible, so a distributed dynamic
coalition mechanism is used. At time
$t$, each subtask $j\in\Xi_k^\ell$ is associated with a coalition
$\mathcal{R}_j(t)\subseteq\mathcal{N}_k$, and the collection of all coalitions
is denoted by
$\overline{\mathcal{R}}_t \triangleq
\{\mathcal{R}_j(t)\mid j\in\Xi_k^\ell\}$.
The coalitions are disjoint and collectively exhaustive, namely
$\mathcal{R}_{j_1}(t)\cap\mathcal{R}_{j_2}(t)=\emptyset$ for
$j_1\neq j_2$, and
$\bigcup_{j\in\Xi_k^\ell}\mathcal{R}_j(t)=\mathcal{N}_k$. The current local
execution plan of robot $i$ is determined by its present coalition assignment,
and its trajectory follows the motion required by that coalition.
To evaluate coalition updates, each robot maintains local estimates of the
coalition costs $\chi(\mathcal{R}_j)$, which depend on robot-target distances
and relative velocities. The instantaneous coordination cost is given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:coalition-cost}
\chi(\overline{\mathcal{R}}_t)\triangleq
\max_{j\in\Xi_k^\ell}\chi(\mathcal{R}_j)
+
\frac{1}{J_k^\ell}\sum_{j\in\Xi_k^\ell}\chi(\mathcal{R}_j),
\end{equation}
where the first term captures the current makespan across subtasks, and the
second promotes balanced workload among coalitions.

The local switch rule is based on whether moving one robot from its current
coalition to a neighboring coalition reduces the worst coalition cost. If robot
$i$ is currently assigned to subtask $j_i$, and $j'$ is a candidate neighboring
subtask, then the switch is accepted only if:
\begin{equation}
\label{ch04:eq:switch-condition}
\max\Bigl\{
\chi(\mathcal{R}_{j'}\cup\{i\}),
\chi(\mathcal{R}_{j_i}\setminus\{i\})
\Bigr\}
<
\max\Bigl\{
\chi(\mathcal{R}_{j_i}),
\chi(\mathcal{R}_{j'})
\Bigr\},
\end{equation}
which is evaluated locally using the current coalition estimates. If
\eqref{ch04:eq:switch-condition} holds, a switch intent is announced to the
relevant neighboring robots, conflicts are resolved by a priority rule, and the
coalition update is synchronized. The local execution plans and trajectories are
then updated accordingly.
The update uses local information exchange and preserves disjoint coalitions.
Its effectiveness depends on sufficiently frequent team connectivity; persistent
disconnection can prevent information propagation, while delays or packet losses
mainly slow convergence and may cause temporary inconsistencies.


\begin{example}
\label{ch04:example:DynamicKnown}
In one dynamic and known
case, five robots are assigned to five moving targets through the distributed
coalition updates. Initially, each
robot is assigned to one target. Later, robots $2$ and $3$ exchange targets,
and once target $3$ is completed, robots $2$ and $4$ form a coalition for
target $2$, while robot $3$ proceeds to target $4$.
\end{example}

The dynamic-and-unknown case is not pursued here. Without knowing the number and
locations of moving subtasks, task completion cannot be certified reliably; such
settings would require combining exploration with dynamic coalition updates.

\begin{theorem}
\label{ch04:thm:local-correctness}
For each task $\omega_k^\ell$ and assigned team $\mathcal{N}_k$, the
coordination strategies in Section~\ref{ch04:subsec:local_coordination}
produce feasible local execution plans and trajectories. In the static and
known case, the local makespan objective is optimized directly. In the static
and unknown case, feasibility is preserved while the increase in execution cost
caused by online insertions is minimized. In the dynamic and known case, the
distributed coalition updates decrease the local coalition cost until a locally
optimal coalition configuration is reached.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
In the static and known case, the subtask set and the subtask locations are
fully specified. For holonomic robots, the coordination problem reduces to
routing with makespan minimization. For non-holonomic robots, the feasible
motion-primitive construction in \eqref{ch04:eq:augmented-cost} ensures that
the resulting trajectories satisfy the robot dynamics while the local objective
is minimized.
In the static and unknown case, exploration guarantees eventual discovery of
all fixed subtasks under complete coverage of the task region. Each discovered
subtask is then inserted by solving \eqref{ch04:eq:min-disruption}, which
preserves feasibility of the current plan while minimizing the incremental cost
of the update. Hence the resulting local execution remains feasible throughout
the exploration process.
In the dynamic and known case, the coalition update rule in
\eqref{ch04:eq:switch-condition} admits a switch only when the worst affected
coalition cost decreases. Therefore, the aggregate coalition cost in
\eqref{ch04:eq:coalition-cost} decreases monotonically under accepted updates.
Since the number of feasible coalition structures over the finite team
$\mathcal{N}_k$ is finite, the update process terminates at a locally optimal
coalition configuration. The associated local plans and trajectories are
therefore feasible and locally consistent with the moving subtasks.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Complexity and scalability analysis}\label{ch04:subsubsec:complexity}

The computational complexity is summarized as follows.
{In each iteration of Algorithm~\ref{ch04:alg:ss-TaskAssign}, selecting a batch of~$P$
nodes and expanding up to~$B \triangleq (K{+}1)\,|\Omega^-_{\nu_h^\star}|$
children incurs $\mathcal{O}(PBK)$ for the automaton-state
updates~$\widehat{Q}^+_m$, together with the capacity and timing
updates in~\eqref{ch04:eq:update-constraint} and~\eqref{ch04:eq:estimated_time};
maintaining the non-dominated frontier based
on~\eqref{ch04:eq:profile}-\eqref{ch04:eq:frontier} requires
$\mathcal{O}(|\mathcal{V}|(2K{+}M))$ to reach
the completeness condition in \eqref{ch04:eq:complete-nodes}. The capacity-based
team-formation MILP introduces $\mathcal{O}(N K^\star)$ binary variables $b_{ik}$,
which is NP-hard in general but limited by the small number of teams.
For the case of static and known tasks, the
routing problem with subtour elimination is NP-hard,
and its number of variables scales with $|\mathcal{N}_k|(J_k^\ell)^2$.
For static and unknown tasks,
polygonal coverage generation and assignment are near-linear in the region
representation, while rolling insertion of newly discovered subtasks is
$\mathcal{O}(|\mathcal{N}_k|)$ per discovery. Lastly, for dynamic and known tasks, the
distributed coalition updates are linear in
team size and the number of active targets per iteration.
Although the
local MILPs and coalition updates are NP-hard in the worst case, the hierarchical
decomposition confines them to small teams, enabling event-based
replanning with standard solvers.
Detailed empirical runtime evaluations of each component and scaling benchmarks
are provided in Section~\ref{sec:ch04_case_study}.}
