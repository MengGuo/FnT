\section{Hierarchical Coordination of Large-Scale Fleets}
\label{sec:hierarchical_coordination}

Temporal-task coordination for large heterogeneous fleets must resolve two
coupled difficulties. The first one is logical. A mission usually prescribes
ordering, eventuality, concurrency, and completion requirements over several
collaborative tasks. The second one is combinatorial. Concrete robots with
different capabilities, current locations, and availability times must be
grouped into teams and deployed over a shared workspace. Monolithic methods
that synchronize temporal automata with all robot models can preserve formal
correctness, but their size grows very rapidly with the number of robots and
tasks
\cite{kantaros2020stylus,schillinger2018simultaneous,luo2022temporal,
leahy2021scalable}. Hierarchical decompositions are therefore essential for
large fleets
\cite{chen2025real,liu2024time,luo2025simultaneous,gosrich2025online,
luo2025hulk}.

This section presents a hierarchy with three layers. The upper layer performs
temporal decomposition and allocates tasks to abstract teams. The intermediate
layer instantiates these abstract teams by assigning concrete robots to
capability profiles. The lower layer realizes each team plan through local
coordination and motion generation. Human-centric protocols are omitted here,
and online adaptation is only touched upon when it clarifies the hierarchy.
The main objective of this section is to formalize how temporal tasks are
converted into executable team-level and robot-level plans.

\subsection{Hierarchical coordination model}
\label{subsec:hierarchical_model}

Let $\mathcal{N} \triangleq \{1,\cdots,N\}$ denote the robot set and let
$\mathcal{W} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be the workspace. For each robot
$i \in \mathcal{N}$, let $\mathcal{A}_i \subseteq \mathcal{A}$ be its action
set, let $x_i \in \mathcal{W}$ be its current position, and let $\tau_i$ be
its timed local plan, consistent with the notation introduced previously.
Furthermore, let
$\Phi_t \triangleq \{\varphi_1,\cdots,\varphi_M\}$ be the mission set known
at time $t$. Each mission is a syntactically co-safe temporal formula over a
finite alphabet of collaborative tasks. Such specifications are standard in
finite-horizon temporal planning because satisfaction can be certified after a
finite trace \cite{baier2008principles}.

\begin{definition}[Collaborative task]
\label{def:collaborative_task_ch4}
A collaborative task is a tuple
\[
\omega \triangleq \big(S_\omega,\eta_\omega,\mathcal{J}_\omega\big),
\]
where $S_\omega \subseteq \mathcal{W}$ is the execution region,
$\eta_\omega$ is an estimated completion-time map, and
\[
\mathcal{J}_\omega \triangleq
\big\{(n_j,a_j,s_j)\, \big| \, j = 1,\cdots,J_\omega \big\}
\]
is the associated subtask set. Here $n_j \in \mathbb{N}$ is the required
number of robots, $a_j \in \mathcal{A}$ is the required action, and
$s_j \in S_\omega$ is the service location of subtask $j$.
\end{definition}

Definition~\ref{def:collaborative_task_ch4} differs from purely counting-based
descriptions in two respects. First, it preserves the spatial support of each
task. Second, it retains the dependence of execution duration on the assigned
team. Both features are important in large fleets, because identical counting
requirements can induce very different response times when robots differ in
location and capability \cite{sahin2019multirobot,cardona2024planning}.

To separate temporal reasoning from robot-level scheduling, the hierarchy uses
team plans together with capability profiles.

\begin{definition}[Abstract team plan and capability profile]
\label{def:team_plan_profile_ch4}
For team index $k \in \mathcal{K}$, an abstract team plan is a finite word
\[
\Gamma_k \triangleq \omega_k^1 \omega_k^2 \cdots \omega_k^{L_k},
\]
where each symbol is a collaborative task. The capability profile induced by
$\Gamma_k$ is the map $\beta_k : \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathbb{N}$ defined
by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:capability_profile_ch4}
\beta_k(a) \triangleq
\max_{\omega \in \Gamma_k}
\max_{\substack{(n_j,a_j,s_j)\in\mathcal{J}_\omega\\ a_j=a}}
n_j,
\end{equation}
where $\beta_k(a)$ is zero if action $a$ does not appear in $\Gamma_k$.
\end{definition}

Equation~\eqref{eq:capability_profile_ch4} captures the peak instantaneous
demand for action $a$ along the team plan. It does not identify which robots
provide that action. This abstraction is crucial, because it decouples the
temporal assignment problem from the subsequent robot-to-team realization.

\subsection{Fleet-level temporal decomposition and assignment}
\label{subsec:fleet_level_assignment}

For each mission $\varphi_m \in \Phi_t$, let
\[
\mathcal{B}_m \triangleq
\big(Q_m,Q_m^0,\Sigma_m,\delta_m,F_m\big)
\]
be the corresponding task automaton, where $Q_m$ is the state set,
$Q_m^0 \subseteq Q_m$ is the initial-state set, $\Sigma_m$ is the task
alphabet, $\delta_m$ is the transition map, and $F_m \subseteq Q_m$ is the
accepting set. The upper layer does not construct the full synchronous product
between all $\mathcal{B}_m$ and all robot models. Instead, it searches over
partial team plans together with the currently reachable automaton states.

\begin{definition}[Assignment node]
\label{def:assignment_node_ch4}
An assignment node is a tuple
\[
\nu \triangleq
\Big(\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}},
\{\widehat{Q}_m\}_{m=1}^M\Big),
\]
where $\Gamma_k$ is the current task word of team $k$, and
$\widehat{Q}_m \subseteq Q_m$ is the set of states reachable in
$\mathcal{B}_m$ after executing the task symbols already assigned in $\nu$.
\end{definition}

Definition~\ref{def:assignment_node_ch4} is the key device that merges
temporal decomposition and team construction. Each node records both the
operational side through $\Gamma_k$ and the logical side through
$\widehat{Q}_m$. Consequently, an expansion step can evaluate not only how a
new task affects resource usage, but also how much it advances each mission
toward acceptance.

The upper-layer problem is stated next.

\begin{problem}
\label{prob:fleet_assignment_ch4}
Given the mission set $\Phi_t$, the associated automata
$\{\mathcal{B}_m\}_{m=1}^M$, and the fleet capability model
$\{\mathcal{A}_i\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}}$, determine the number of teams
$K \triangleq |\mathcal{K}|$, the abstract team plans
$\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$, and the corresponding capability profiles
$\{\beta_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$ such that all missions are satisfiable and
the fleet response time is minimized.
\end{problem}

Let $T_k$ be the estimated completion time of $\Gamma_k$ and let $C_k$ be the
associated travel-and-service cost. For each mission $m$, let
$\psi_m(q,F_m)$ denote the shortest graph distance from $q \in Q_m$ to the
accepting set $F_m$. Then each node $\nu$ is ranked by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:node_value_ch4}
\chi(\nu) \triangleq
\max_{k\in\mathcal{K}} T_k
+ \lambda_1 \sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}} C_k
+ \lambda_2 \sum_{m=1}^M
w_m \min_{q\in\widehat{Q}_m}\psi_m(q,F_m),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_1 > 0$ and $\lambda_2 > 0$ are regularization parameters and
$w_m > 0$ is the priority weight of mission $\varphi_m$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:node_value_ch4} balances three criteria. The first term
penalizes large makespan. The second term discourages unnecessarily long team
routes. The third term favors nodes that are logically closer to acceptance.
This combination is useful in large problem instances, because nodes with
similar completion times can still differ significantly in mission progress.

The capability profile of each node must also satisfy a fleet-level feasibility
test:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fleet_capacity_ch4}
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}} \beta_k(a)
\leq
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}} \mathds{1}\big(a\in\mathcal{A}_i\big),
\quad \forall a \in \mathcal{A},
\end{equation}
where the left-hand side is the aggregate demand induced by the current team
plans, and the right-hand side is the number of robots in the fleet that can
execute action $a$. By \eqref{eq:fleet_capacity_ch4}, the upper layer never
produces an abstract assignment that is impossible to realize in terms of
capability counts.

A candidate expansion must also be temporally admissible. Given node $\nu$,
the enabled task set is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:enabled_task_set_ch4}
\Omega(\nu) \triangleq
\bigcup_{m=1}^M
\Big\{
\omega \in \Sigma_m \,\Big|\, \exists q \in \widehat{Q}_m
\text{ such that } \delta_m(q,\omega) \neq \emptyset
\Big\}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:enabled_task_set_ch4} identifies the task symbols that can
extend at least one mission automaton along a valid transition.

Suppose that $\omega \in \Omega(\nu)$ is appended to team $k$. The updated
node is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:node_update_ch4}
\nu^+ \triangleq
\Big(
\Gamma_1,\cdots,\Gamma_k\omega,\cdots,\Gamma_K,
\widehat{Q}_1^+,\cdots,\widehat{Q}_M^+
\Big),
\end{equation}
where
\[
\widehat{Q}_m^+ \triangleq
\bigcup_{q\in\widehat{Q}_m} \delta_m(q,\omega),
\quad m = 1,\cdots,M.
\]
Thus, \eqref{eq:node_update_ch4} advances the operational plan and the
mission-automaton states simultaneously.

To prune inferior nodes, define the profile
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dominance_profile_ch4}
\rho(\nu) \triangleq
\Big[
\{T_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}},
\{C_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}},
\Big\{
\min_{q\in\widehat{Q}_m}\psi_m(q,F_m)
\Big\}_{m=1}^M
\Big].
\end{equation}
Node $\nu_1$ dominates node $\nu_2$ if $\rho(\nu_1)$ is no larger than
$\rho(\nu_2)$ componentwise and is strictly smaller in at least one entry.
The frontier therefore contains only non-dominated nodes. This pruning rule
greatly reduces redundant exploration, and it is one of the main reasons why
the hierarchical search remains tractable for large fleets
\cite{chen2025real,liu2024time,luo2025hulk}.

\begin{algorithm}[t!]
\caption{Automaton-Guided Task Decomposition and Team Assignment}
\label{alg:hierarchical_assignment_ch4}
\SetKwInOut{Input}{Input}
\SetKwInOut{Output}{Output}
\Input{Mission set $\Phi_t = \{\varphi_1,\cdots,\varphi_M\}$, automata
$\{\mathcal{B}_m\}_{m=1}^M$, fleet $\mathcal{N}$, planning horizon $H$, batch
size $P$.}
\Output{Team index set $\mathcal{K}$, abstract team plans
$\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$, capability profiles
$\{\beta_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$.}

Initialize $\mathcal{K} \gets \{1\}$ and $\Gamma_1 \gets \emptyset$\;
Initialize the root node
$\nu_0 \gets \big(\{\emptyset\},\{Q_m^0\}_{m=1}^M\big)$\;
Initialize the frontier $\mathcal{F} \gets \{\nu_0\}$\;

\While{the computational budget is not exhausted}{
    Select a subset $\mathcal{V}_P \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ with
    $|\mathcal{V}_P| = P$ that minimizes $\chi(\nu)$ in
    \eqref{eq:node_value_ch4}\;

    \ForEach{$\nu \in \mathcal{V}_P$}{
        Compute the enabled set $\Omega(\nu)$ from
        \eqref{eq:enabled_task_set_ch4}\;

        \ForEach{$\omega \in \Omega(\nu)$}{
            Consider all admissible team indices in
            $\mathcal{K} \cup \{K+1\}$\;

            \ForEach{candidate team index $k$}{
                Append $\omega$ to the corresponding team word\;
                Update the reachable-state sets by
                \eqref{eq:node_update_ch4}\;
                Update the profile $\beta_k(\cdot)$ by
                \eqref{eq:capability_profile_ch4}\;
                Update the timing and cost estimates
                $T_k$ and $C_k$\;

                \If{\eqref{eq:fleet_capacity_ch4} is violated}{
                    discard the candidate node\;
                }

                \If{the number of committed future tasks reaches $H$}{
                    mark the node as paused\;
                }

                add each feasible child node to $\mathcal{F}$\;
            }
        }
    }

    Remove dominated nodes using \eqref{eq:dominance_profile_ch4}\;
    Retain only the non-dominated frontier nodes\;
}

Select a complete node that minimizes \eqref{eq:node_value_ch4}\;
Return the induced $\mathcal{K}$, $\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$, and
$\{\beta_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$\;
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:hierarchical_assignment_ch4} makes the hierarchical logic
explicit. A node is selected according to \eqref{eq:node_value_ch4}, expanded
only through symbols in \eqref{eq:enabled_task_set_ch4}, filtered by the
capability test in \eqref{eq:fleet_capacity_ch4}, and finally compared through
the dominance profile in \eqref{eq:dominance_profile_ch4}. The planning
horizon $H$ limits only the amount of future commitment in one cycle. It does
not alter the semantics of the task automata.

\subsection{Capability-based team formation}
\label{subsec:team_formation_ch4}

After the upper layer returns the abstract team plans, the next layer assigns
concrete robots to these plans. This layer is necessary because
Definition~\ref{def:team_plan_profile_ch4} specifies only how many robots of
each capability a team needs. It does not determine which robots should form
that team. The resulting realization problem is much smaller than direct
robot-to-subtask assignment, because it operates at the level of capability
profiles rather than individual subtasks.

\begin{definition}[Concrete team realization]
\label{def:concrete_team_realization_ch4}
A concrete realization of the abstract team plans
$\{\Gamma_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$ is a collection
$\{\mathcal{N}_k\}_{k\in\mathcal{K}}$ such that
$\mathcal{N}_k \subseteq \mathcal{N}$ for each $k$ and
$\mathcal{N}_{k_1} \cap \mathcal{N}_{k_2} = \emptyset$ whenever
$k_1 \neq k_2$.
\end{definition}

Let $b_{ik} \in \{0,1\}$ be the binary variable that indicates whether robot
$i$ is assigned to team $k$. Let $\alpha_a \geq 1$ be the redundancy factor
associated with action $a$. Then the realization constraints are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:team_realization_ch4}
\beta_k(a)
\leq
\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}} b_{ik}\mathds{1}\big(a\in\mathcal{A}_i\big)
\leq
\alpha_a \beta_k(a),
\quad
\sum_{k\in\mathcal{K}} b_{ik} \leq 1,
\end{equation}
where the first inequality enforces the minimum and maximum staffing level for
action $a$, and the second inequality guarantees disjoint membership.

Equation~\eqref{eq:team_realization_ch4} has two consequences. The lower bound
ensures that every team can execute all tasks in its abstract word. The upper
bound avoids over-concentration of robots in one team. This redundancy margin
is valuable when task duration, hidden subtasks, or travel time are uncertain.
It also preserves flexibility for future reallocation.

Let $S_k^1$ be the region of the first task in $\Gamma_k$. For robot $i$ and
team $k$, define the arrival estimate
\[
t_{ik}^{\mathrm{arr}} \triangleq
\widehat{t}_i + T_{\mathrm{nav}}(\widehat{x}_i,S_k^1),
\]
where $\widehat{t}_i$ and $\widehat{x}_i$ are the current availability time
and position of robot $i$. Then the predicted completion time of team $k$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:team_completion_ch4}
J_k \triangleq
\max_{i\,:\,b_{ik}=1} t_{ik}^{\mathrm{arr}}
+
\sum_{\ell=1}^{L_k} T_{\mathrm{exec}}(\omega_k^\ell),
\end{equation}
where the first term is the synchronization delay induced by the slowest
assigned robot, and the second term is the cumulative service time along the
team word. The team-formation layer minimizes $\max_{k\in\mathcal{K}} J_k$
subject to \eqref{eq:team_realization_ch4}.

Once the binary solution is obtained, the concrete team is recovered by
\[
\mathcal{N}_k \triangleq
\{ i \in \mathcal{N} \mid b_{ik} = 1 \}.
\]
Each robot in $\mathcal{N}_k$ then inherits the team rollout
\[
\Xi_k \triangleq
(S_k^1,\omega_k^1)\cdots(S_k^{L_k},\omega_k^{L_k}),
\]
which serves as the interface to the lower layer. Therefore, the output of the
intermediate layer is a physically realizable partition of the fleet together
with an ordered team-level task sequence.

\subsection{Execution-level realization within each team}
\label{subsec:local_task_coordination_ch4}

The lower layer refines each team rollout $\Xi_k$ into timed robot plans
$\{\tau_i\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}$ and associated trajectories. At this stage,
the team index $k$ is fixed, the team composition $\mathcal{N}_k$ is fixed,
and the task sequence $\Gamma_k$ is fixed. What remains is to decide how the
robots in $\mathcal{N}_k$ should distribute the subtasks of each
$\omega_k^\ell$ and how they should move in the workspace. This stage is not a
single optimization template, because the structure of the local problem
depends strongly on whether subtasks are known or hidden, and whether they are
static or moving. Three cases are particularly important.

\subsubsection{Static and known subtasks}
\label{subsubsec:static_known_ch4}

Consider a task $\omega_k^\ell$ for which the entire set
$\mathcal{J}_{k,\ell} \triangleq \mathcal{J}_{\omega_k^\ell}$ is known before
execution and every service location $s_j$ is fixed. This situation arises in
delivery, inspection, and transport tasks over a predetermined set of sites.
In that case, the local coordination problem reduces to assigning the known
subtasks to the robots in $\mathcal{N}_k$ and constructing feasible routes
with small makespan.

Let $\sigma_i$ denote the ordered list of subtasks assigned to robot $i$, and
let $\mathcal{P}(\sigma_i)$ be the corresponding geometric path. For
holonomic robots, the path length is determined essentially by the ordering of
the visited subtasks. For non-holonomic robots, the heading and curvature
constraints also matter, so route sequencing and motion synthesis must be
solved jointly. A suitable objective is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:static_known_cost_ch4}
T_{k,\ell}^{\mathrm{SK}} \triangleq
\min_{\{\sigma_i,\mathcal{P}(\sigma_i)\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}}
\max_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}
\Big(
T_{\mathrm{nav}}(\mathcal{P}(\sigma_i))
+
T_{\mathrm{srv}}(\sigma_i)
\Big),
\end{equation}
where $T_{\mathrm{nav}}(\mathcal{P}(\sigma_i))$ is the travel time along the
path of robot $i$, and $T_{\mathrm{srv}}(\sigma_i)$ is the cumulative service
time of the subtasks in $\sigma_i$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:static_known_cost_ch4} is the local makespan criterion for
the static-known case. When robots are fully actuated, the main difficulty is
combinatorial assignment and ordering, closely related to multi-vehicle
routing. When robots obey curvature or heading constraints, the path term in
\eqref{eq:static_known_cost_ch4} must be computed over dynamically feasible
motion primitives. In that case, each transition between two service states is
assigned the minimum feasible travel cost, which leads to a hybrid discrete and
continuous optimization problem
\cite{ny2011dubins,vavna2015dubins,erke2020improved}. The lower layer then
returns one timed sequence $\tau_i$ for each robot together with feasible
trajectories that realize this sequence.

\subsubsection{Static and partially revealed subtasks}
\label{subsubsec:static_unknown_ch4}

The second case arises when the task region is fixed but the subtask set is
not fully known at the time the team enters the region. Search-and-rescue and
surveillance with hidden points of interest are typical examples. Let
$\widehat{\mathcal{J}}_{k,\ell}(r)$ denote the set of subtasks discovered up to
update index $r$, where $r = 0$ corresponds to the moment when the team
starts exploring $S_k^\ell$. The local problem has two tightly coupled parts.
The first one is coverage of the region. The second one is insertion of newly
discovered subtasks into the active robot routes.

The initial exploration paths should distribute the sensing burden over the
team while accounting for robot positions, sensor footprints, and obstacles.
Let $\pi_i^r$ be the exploration-and-service path of robot $i$ after update
$r$. When a new subtask is discovered, the lower layer evaluates where it
should be inserted so that the increase in team makespan remains small. A
useful criterion is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:static_unknown_update_ch4}
\Delta T_{k,\ell}^{\mathrm{SU}}(r)
\triangleq
\min_{\{\pi_i^{r+1}\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}}
\Bigg[
\max_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k} T(\pi_i^{r+1})
-
\max_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k} T(\pi_i^r)
\Bigg],
\end{equation}
where $T(\pi_i^r)$ is the predicted completion time of robot $i$ after the
$r$th discovery stage.

Equation~\eqref{eq:static_unknown_update_ch4} quantifies the minimum increase
in team completion time due to newly revealed work. The optimization is local
in the sense that the current team $\mathcal{N}_k$ and the current team word
remain unchanged. Only the internal realization of the current task is
modified. This property is important for the hierarchy, because hidden
subtasks should not force a re-solution of the fleet-level assignment whenever
a team discovers additional work.

In obstacle-free regions, one may begin with a coverage decomposition of
$S_k^\ell$ and then allocate portions of the area to robots according to
position and sensing range. In cluttered environments, frontier-based
exploration is often more appropriate \cite{holz2010evaluating}. In both
settings, the discovered subtasks are inserted into the robot routes by
evaluating the marginal increase in \eqref{eq:static_unknown_update_ch4}. The
result is a collection of time-varying local plans that interleave exploration
and service until the region has been covered and all discovered subtasks have
been executed.

\subsubsection{Dynamic and known subtasks}
\label{subsubsec:dynamic_known_ch4}

The third case concerns moving subtasks whose number is known initially and
whose positions are updated online through sensing or tracking. Dynamic capture
and cooperative escort are typical examples. The main difficulty is that any
static allocation can become inefficient or infeasible as the targets move.
The lower layer therefore requires online coalition adaptation inside the team.

Let
\[
\mathcal{R}_{k,\ell}(t) \triangleq
\{R_{k,\ell}^1(t),\cdots,R_{k,\ell}^{J_{k,\ell}}(t)\}
\]
be the coalition scheme at time $t$, where $R_{k,\ell}^j(t)$ is the subset of
robots assigned to moving subtask $j$. The subsets form a partition of
$\mathcal{N}_k$. Let $\chi_j\big(R_{k,\ell}^j(t),t\big)$ denote the predicted
completion cost of coalition $R_{k,\ell}^j(t)$ for target $j$. A convenient
team-level performance index is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dynamic_team_cost_ch4}
\chi_{k,\ell}(t) \triangleq
\max_{1\leq j \leq J_{k,\ell}}
\chi_j\big(R_{k,\ell}^j(t),t\big)
+
\frac{1}{J_{k,\ell}}
\sum_{j=1}^{J_{k,\ell}}
\chi_j\big(R_{k,\ell}^j(t),t\big).
\end{equation}

The first term in \eqref{eq:dynamic_team_cost_ch4} controls the local
makespan. The second term regularizes load balancing across moving subtasks.
Suppose robot $i$ currently belongs to coalition $R_{k,\ell}^{j_i}(t)$ and
considers switching to coalition $R_{k,\ell}^{j'}(t)$. A locally beneficial
switch is detected when
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:switch_rule_ch4}
\max
\Big\{
\chi_{j'}\big(R_{k,\ell}^{j'}(t)\cup\{i\},t\big),
\chi_{j_i}\big(R_{k,\ell}^{j_i}(t)\setminus\{i\},t\big)
\Big\}
<
\max
\Big\{
\chi_{j'}\big(R_{k,\ell}^{j'}(t),t\big),
\chi_{j_i}\big(R_{k,\ell}^{j_i}(t),t\big)
\Big\}.
\end{equation}

Equation~\eqref{eq:switch_rule_ch4} states that the worst coalition cost among
the two affected subtasks must decrease after the switch. This is a purely
local rule, yet it is consistent with the makespan-oriented structure of
\eqref{eq:dynamic_team_cost_ch4}. It is therefore appropriate for distributed
updates inside a team.

\begin{algorithm}[t!]
\caption{Distributed Coalition Update for Dynamic Known Subtasks}
\label{alg:dynamic_coalition_ch4}
\SetKwInOut{Input}{Input}
\SetKwInOut{Output}{Output}
\Input{Team $\mathcal{N}_k$, moving subtasks
$\mathcal{J}_{k,\ell} = \{1,\cdots,J_{k,\ell}\}$, current coalition scheme
$\mathcal{R}_{k,\ell}(t)$, local coalition costs
$\chi_j\big(R_{k,\ell}^j(t),t\big)$.}
\Output{Updated coalition scheme $\mathcal{R}_{k,\ell}(t^+)$ and local
plans $\{\tau_i\}_{i\in\mathcal{N}_k}$.}

\Repeat{no robot can improve its local assignment}{
    \ForEach{robot $i \in \mathcal{N}_k$}{
        identify the current subtask index $j_i$\;
        estimate neighboring coalition costs for admissible targets $j'$\;

        \If{\eqref{eq:switch_rule_ch4} holds for some $j'$}{
            announce a switch request from $j_i$ to $j'$\;
            resolve simultaneous requests by a local priority rule\;
            update the affected coalitions
            $R_{k,\ell}^{j_i}(t)$ and $R_{k,\ell}^{j'}(t)$\;
            update the local timed plan $\tau_i$\;
        }
    }
}

Return the updated coalition scheme and the corresponding local plans\;
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:dynamic_coalition_ch4} does not modify the team-level task
word. It only redistributes the robots of the current team across the moving
subtasks of the current task. Hence the hierarchy is preserved. The upper
layer determines which team executes the task. The lower layer determines how
that team should adapt internally as the targets move.

\subsection{Discussion on hierarchical consistency}
\label{subsec:hierarchical_consistency_ch4}

The hierarchy developed above has a precise division of responsibilities. The
fleet-level layer decides which task symbols should be assigned and in what
order, while preserving temporal correctness through the automata states in
Definition~\ref{def:assignment_node_ch4}. The intermediate layer instantiates
the resulting abstract plans through the capability constraints in
\eqref{eq:team_realization_ch4}. The lower layer solves only the internal
realization problem of each team.

This separation is important for scalability. Hidden subtasks in
Subsection~\ref{subsubsec:static_unknown_ch4} modify only the internal service
paths of the current team. Dynamic targets in
Subsection~\ref{subsubsec:dynamic_known_ch4} modify only the coalition scheme
inside that team. Even in the static-known case of
Subsection~\ref{subsubsec:static_known_ch4}, the routing computation is carried
out after the temporal allocation has already been fixed. As a consequence, the
full synchronous product of all robot models with all task automata is never
constructed. This is precisely the structural advantage that makes temporal
coordination of large fleets computationally viable
\cite{kantaros2020stylus,schillinger2018simultaneous,chen2025real,
luo2025simultaneous,gosrich2025online,luo2025hulk}.
