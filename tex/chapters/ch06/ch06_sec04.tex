\subsection{Reactive coordination under dynamic temporal tasks}
\label{ch06:subsec:case_dynamic_tasks}

This case study illustrates the reactive coordination framework
developed in Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_uncertain}. The emphasis is on the
interaction among target-motion prediction from
Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:traj-predictor}, conformal prediction regions
from Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:cpr}, R-poset abstraction from
Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:poset}, uncertainty-aware task assignment from
Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:assignment}, and the online adaptation
mechanism governed by \eqref{ch06:eq:task-update} and
\eqref{ch06:eq:update_eta}. The environment is a city-like road network
with static obstacles and multiple moving targets.

\subsubsection{Scenario and task specification}
\label{ch06:subsubsec:case_dynamic_tasks_scenario}

The team consists of $12$ UAVs, divided into tracking UAVs and
interception UAVs. Three suspects move through the urban area with
different mobility patterns, namely one on foot, one on bike, and one
by car. These suspects are treated as dynamic targets in the sense of
Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:robots}, while the scouting regions are
modeled as static targets with zero velocity. The future motion of the
suspects is estimated by the predictor in
Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:traj-predictor}, and the associated
uncertainty is incorporated through the conformal regions in
Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:cpr}.
The mission combines an offline task component with online reactive
task releases. Initially, the team is required to scout two designated
regions and to complete track-before-intercept tasks for the three
suspects. Using the notation of
Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:task-specification}, the initial task formula
is specified by:
\begin{equation}
\varphi_{\texttt{static}}
=
\Diamond \texttt{scout}_{s_1}
\wedge
\Diamond \texttt{scout}_{s_2}
\wedge
\bigwedge_{\xi \in \{\texttt{f},\texttt{b},\texttt{c}\}}
\Diamond\bigl(
\texttt{track}_{\xi}
\wedge
\neg \texttt{intercept}_{\xi}
\wedge
\Diamond \texttt{intercept}_{\xi}
\bigr),
\label{ch06:eq:case_dynamic_static}
\end{equation}
where $\texttt{scout}_{s_i}$ denotes the scouting task in region
$s_i$, and $\texttt{track}_{\xi}$ and
$\texttt{intercept}_{\xi}$ denote the tracking and interception tasks
for suspect $\xi \in \{\texttt{f},\texttt{b},\texttt{c}\}$.


During execution, two additional scouting tasks are released online.
According to the update rule in \eqref{ch06:eq:task-update}, these
reactive tasks are appended to the current formula as:
\begin{equation}
\varphi_{\texttt{r}}(t_1)
=
\Diamond \texttt{scout}_{s_3},
\qquad
\varphi_{\texttt{r}}(t_2)
=
\Diamond \texttt{scout}_{s_4},
\label{ch06:eq:case_dynamic_reactive}
\end{equation}
where $t_1$ and $t_2$ are the release times of the new scouting tasks
in regions $s_3$ and $s_4$, respectively.
Figure~\ref{ch06:fig:case_dynamic_tasks_urban_setup} shows the main
simulation setup. The left panel presents the initial scene, including
the three suspects, the designated scouting regions, and the initial
deployment of the UAV team. The right panel shows the overall
trajectories generated during execution. In this scenario, the task
formula in \eqref{ch06:eq:case_dynamic_static}, together with the
reactive updates in \eqref{ch06:eq:case_dynamic_reactive}, is abstracted
into an R-poset as in Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:poset}. The resulting
precedence structure is then used by the CP-based Monte Carlo tree
search in Subsubsec.~\ref{ch06:sec:cp-mcts} to generate and revise team
assignments under predicted target motion and uncertainty.


\subsubsection{Execution results and online adaptation}
\label{ch06:subsubsec:case_dynamic_tasks_results}

Figure~\ref{ch06:fig:case_dynamic_tasks_timeline} reports the execution
timeline in the form of a Gantt chart. The initial plan is synthesized
from the offline component \eqref{ch06:eq:case_dynamic_static}. The
mission contains $10$ tasks in total, namely $6$ offline tasks and
$4$ reactive scouting tasks. During execution, the task assignments are
updated at $t=40\,\mathrm{s}$ and $t=80\,\mathrm{s}$ when new reactive
tasks are released, in accordance with \eqref{ch06:eq:task-update}.
Between these events, performance-triggered replanning checks are also
carried out at $t=64\,\mathrm{s}$, $t=79\,\mathrm{s}$, and
$t=104\,\mathrm{s}$ according to \eqref{ch06:eq:update_eta}. These
checks invoke the planner again, but the maintained schedule is
preserved whenever the candidate solution does not improve the predicted
average makespan in \eqref{ch06:eq:avg-makespan}. Hence, after the
initial offline synthesis, the planner is called online $5$ times, of
which $2$ calls lead to actual assignment updates and $3$ calls retain
the current schedule.

\begin{figure}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]%
{figures/ch06/case_dynamic_tasks_timeline.png}
\caption{
Execution timeline for the reactive coordination scenario. The bars
show task execution intervals for the tracking and interception UAVs.
The dashed markers indicate the initial planning instant,

}
\label{ch06:fig:case_dynamic_tasks_timeline}
\end{figure}

The timeline confirms the intended behavior of the framework. The
initial search produces a feasible assignment for the offline task set.
When a new scouting task is released, the task formula is updated, the
remaining subtasks are re-abstracted through the R-poset construction,
and the assignment is revised accordingly. When replanning is triggered
only by degradation in the predicted quality of the current plan, the
planner may return a candidate solution that is inferior to the
currently maintained one; in that case, the existing schedule is
retained. Numerically, the schedule-change ratio of online replanning is
therefore $2/5=40\%$, while $60\%$ of the online planner calls serve as
consistency checks that do not alter the maintained assignment. This
behavior shows that the event-triggering mechanism is sufficiently
responsive to evolving conditions, but does not introduce unnecessary
plan churn.
The system terminates at $t=136\,\mathrm{s}$ and completes all
$10$ tasks with an average makespan of $66.9\,\mathrm{s}$. Since
$4$ of the $10$ tasks are reactive scouting tasks, the online component
accounts for $40\%$ of the total mission workload. Moreover, the ratio
between the average makespan and the total mission duration is
approximately $66.9/136 \approx 0.492$, which indicates that the team
maintains a substantial degree of parallelism instead of resolving the
tasks in a nearly sequential manner. From the Gantt chart, the busiest
stage occurs around $t=77\,\mathrm{s}$ to $t=82\,\mathrm{s}$, where
about $7$ UAVs are active simultaneously, including approximately
$2$ tracking UAVs and $5$ interception UAVs. This concurrent activity
is consistent with the objective of the proposed planner, namely to
preserve logical feasibility while exploiting parallel execution
whenever the task structure permits it.

\subsection{Collaborative optimal transport under signal temporal tasks}
\label{ch06:subsec:case_stl}

This case study reports representative simulation results for the
collaborative optimal transport framework developed in
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_stl}. The aim is to illustrate how the batched
robustness evaluation in Section~\ref{ch06:subsec:batch-stl}, the
sequential local transport updates in
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:local}, and the hybrid search procedure in
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:hybrid} operate together on collaborative STL
tasks of moderate scale.
In all simulations, the robot radius is set to
$r = 0.1\,\mathrm{m}$, the discrete time step is
$dt = 0.1\,\mathrm{s}$, and the planning horizon is
$T = 48$. The workspace is defined on a
$14 \times 9$ planar domain with static obstacles. For the local
optimal transport updates, the batch size is fixed at
$400$ trajectories. The resulting trajectories and robustness traces are
shown in Figures.~\ref{ch06:fig:split-merge} and
\ref{ch06:fig:alternative-visitation}.

\subsubsection{Split-and-merge navigation under staged connectivity}
\label{ch06:subsubsec:case_split_merge}

The first scenario considers a team of $9$ robots navigating in an
obstacle-cluttered environment. The task consists of two stages. In the
first stage, the team is partitioned into three subgroups, and each
subgroup is required to visit its designated target region while
maintaining an intra-group chain constraint with threshold
$0.8\,\mathrm{m}$. In the second stage, the three subgroups merge into a
single connected team and then pass through a narrow corridor to reach
the final target region.
Let $\mu_{A_j}$ denote the predicate that subgroup $j$ enters its
designated target region. Let $\mu_{\textup{chain}}^j$ denote the chain
predicate for subgroup $j$, and let $\mu_{\textup{conn}}$ denote the
predicate that the whole team remains connected. The corresponding STL
task is given by:
\begin{equation}
\phi_1
=
\left(
\bigwedge_{j=1}^{3}\mathsf{F}_{[0,2.4]}\mu_{A_j}
\right)
\wedge
\left(
\bigwedge_{j=1}^{3}\mathsf{G}_{[0,2.4]}
\mu_{\textup{chain}}^j
\right)
\wedge
\mathsf{G}_{[2.4,4.7]}\mu_{\textup{conn}},
\label{ch06:eq:case_split_merge}
\end{equation}
where the first two terms encode the split phase, and the last term
imposes global connectivity during the merge-and-traverse phase.

Figure~\ref{ch06:fig:split-merge} shows a representative execution. The
left panel presents the planned trajectories. The three subgroups first
separate to visit their intermediate target regions and then merge into
a connected formation before entering the narrow passage. The right
panel shows the corresponding predicate robustness values. The regional
predicates $\mu_{A_1}$, $\mu_{A_2}$, and $\mu_{A_3}$ become positive
during the first stage, the three chain predicates remain positive on
$[0,2.4]$, and the global connectivity predicate becomes positive after
the switching time and stays positive thereafter. In this
representative trial, a feasible plan is obtained in
$26.13\,\mathrm{s}$.

\subsubsection{Alternative target visitation under persistent chain constraints}
\label{ch06:subsubsec:case_alternative_visitation}

The second scenario considers a team of $12$ robots moving through a
workspace with non-convex obstacles. In this case, the team must
maintain a chain formation throughout the whole motion, with a
constraint margin of $1\,\mathrm{m}$. In addition, two visitation
objectives are imposed over the full horizon. For each objective, two
candidate target regions are given, and satisfying either one is
sufficient.
Let $\mu_A^{i,j}$ denote the predicate that all robots simultaneously
enter the~$j$-th candidate region of the~$i$-th visitation objective, and
let $\mu_{\textup{chain}}$ denote the chain predicate for the whole
team. The corresponding STL task is
\begin{equation}
\phi_2
=
\bigwedge_{i=1}^{2}
\left(
\bigvee_{j=1}^{2}\mathsf{F}_{[0,4.7]}\mu_A^{i,j}
\right)
\wedge
\mathsf{G}_{[0,4.7]}\mu_{\textup{chain}},
\label{ch06:eq:case_alternative_visitation}
\end{equation}
where each disjunction encodes one alternative visitation objective,
while the last term enforces the chain constraint over the entire
horizon.
Figure~\ref{ch06:fig:alternative-visitation} shows a representative
solution. The left panel indicates that the team preserves the chain
structure while navigating around the non-convex obstacles and
eventually reaches one admissible target region from each objective
pair. The right panel reports the associated robustness traces. The
chain robustness remains positive throughout the whole horizon, and one
predicate in each disjunctive visitation set becomes positive, thereby
confirming satisfaction of
\eqref{ch06:eq:case_alternative_visitation}. In the representative
trial, the team selects the lower candidate region in both objective
sets, and the planning time is $36.97\,\mathrm{s}$.

These two examples are consistent with the analytical development in
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_stl}. The first emphasizes staged task logic
together with time-dependent connectivity requirements, whereas the
second highlights disjunctive region visitation under persistent
coupling. In both cases, the robustness traces agree with the formal STL
specifications, and the resulting trajectories demonstrate that the
combined use of batched robustness evaluation, sequential local
transport, and hybrid search yields feasible collaborative plans in
geometrically nontrivial environments.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch06_summary}

Chapter~\ref{chap:uncertainty} has examined how dynamic constraints and
environmental uncertainty reshape multi-robot planning at several
levels of abstraction.
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_safe_plan} addressed probabilistic temporal
tasks under safety and safe-return requirements through hierarchical
abstractions and policy synthesis.
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_uncertain} then considered reactive
coordination under dynamic temporal tasks, emphasizing online adaptation
to moving targets and evolving task information.
Finally, Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_stl} developed a continuous
trajectory-optimization perspective based on collaborative optimal
transport, in which STL robustness, geometric safety, and inter-robot
couplings are incorporated into a unified planning framework.
Taken together, these sections show that effective multi-robot planning
under uncertainty requires the coordinated use of symbolic abstraction,
reactive replanning, and continuous optimization, depending on whether
the dominant challenge lies in probabilistic task evolution, dynamic
task geometry, or tightly coupled motion synthesis.
