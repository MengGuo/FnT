\subsection{Collaborative optimal transport under signal temporal tasks}
\label{ch06:subsec:case_stl}

This case study reports representative simulation results for the
collaborative optimal transport framework developed in
Sec.~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_stl}. The aim is to illustrate how the batched
robustness evaluation in Subsec.~\ref{ch06:subsec:batch-stl}, the
sequential local transport updates in
Subsec.~\ref{ch06:sec:local}, and the hybrid search procedure in
Subsec.~\ref{ch06:sec:hybrid} operate together on collaborative STL
tasks of moderate scale.

In all simulations, the robot radius is set to
$r \triangleq 0.1\,\mathrm{m}$, the discrete time step is
$dt \triangleq 0.1\,\mathrm{s}$, and the planning horizon is
$T \triangleq 48$. The workspace is defined on a
$14 \times 9$ planar domain with static obstacles. For the local
optimal transport updates, the batch size is fixed at
$400$ trajectories. The resulting trajectories and robustness traces are
shown in Figs.~\ref{ch06:fig:split-merge} and
\ref{ch06:fig:alternative-visitation}.

\subsubsection{Split-and-merge navigation under staged connectivity}
\label{ch06:subsubsec:case_split_merge}

The first scenario considers a team of $9$ robots navigating in an
obstacle-cluttered environment. The task consists of two stages. In the
first stage, the team is partitioned into three subgroups, and each
subgroup is required to visit its designated target region while
maintaining an intra-group chain constraint with threshold
$0.8\,\mathrm{m}$. In the second stage, the three subgroups merge into a
single connected team and then pass through a narrow corridor to reach
the final target region.
Let $\mu_{A_j}$ denote the predicate that subgroup $j$ enters its
designated target region. Let $\mu_{\textup{chain}}^j$ denote the chain
predicate for subgroup $j$, and let $\mu_{\textup{conn}}$ denote the
predicate that the whole team remains connected. The corresponding STL
task is
\begin{equation}
\phi_1
=
\left(
\bigwedge_{j=1}^{3}\mathsf{F}_{[0,2.4]}\mu_{A_j}
\right)
\wedge
\left(
\bigwedge_{j=1}^{3}\mathsf{G}_{[0,2.4]}
\mu_{\textup{chain}}^j
\right)
\wedge
\mathsf{G}_{[2.4,4.7]}\mu_{\textup{conn}},
\label{ch06:eq:case_split_merge}
\end{equation}
where the first two terms encode the split phase, and the last term
imposes global connectivity during the merge-and-traverse phase.

Figure~\ref{ch06:fig:split-merge} shows a representative execution. The
left panel presents the planned trajectories. The three subgroups first
separate to visit their intermediate target regions and then merge into
a connected formation before entering the narrow passage. The right
panel shows the corresponding predicate robustness values. The regional
predicates $\mu_{A_1}$, $\mu_{A_2}$, and $\mu_{A_3}$ become positive
during the first stage, the three chain predicates remain positive on
$[0,2.4]$, and the global connectivity predicate becomes positive after
the switching time and stays positive thereafter. In this
representative trial, a feasible plan is obtained in
$26.13\,\mathrm{s}$.

\subsubsection{Alternative target visitation under persistent chain
constraints}
\label{ch06:subsubsec:case_alternative_visitation}

The second scenario considers a team of $12$ robots moving through a
workspace with non-convex obstacles. In this case, the team must
maintain a chain formation throughout the whole motion, with a
constraint margin of $1\,\mathrm{m}$. In addition, two visitation
objectives are imposed over the full horizon. For each objective, two
candidate target regions are given, and satisfying either one is
sufficient.
Let $\mu_A^{i,j}$ denote the predicate that all robots simultaneously
enter the $j$th candidate region of the $i$th visitation objective, and
let $\mu_{\textup{chain}}$ denote the chain predicate for the whole
team. The corresponding STL task is
\begin{equation}
\phi_2
=
\bigwedge_{i=1}^{2}
\left(
\bigvee_{j=1}^{2}\mathsf{F}_{[0,4.7]}\mu_A^{i,j}
\right)
\wedge
\mathsf{G}_{[0,4.7]}\mu_{\textup{chain}},
\label{ch06:eq:case_alternative_visitation}
\end{equation}
where each disjunction encodes one alternative visitation objective,
while the last term enforces the chain constraint over the entire
horizon.

Figure~\ref{ch06:fig:alternative-visitation} shows a representative
solution. The left panel indicates that the team preserves the chain
structure while navigating around the non-convex obstacles and
eventually reaches one admissible target region from each objective
pair. The right panel reports the associated robustness traces. The
chain robustness remains positive throughout the whole horizon, and one
predicate in each disjunctive visitation set becomes positive, thereby
confirming satisfaction of
\eqref{ch06:eq:case_alternative_visitation}. In the representative
trial, the team selects the lower candidate region in both objective
sets, and the planning time is $36.97\,\mathrm{s}$.

\begin{figure}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]%

\caption{

}

\end{figure}

These two examples are consistent with the analytical development in
Sec.~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_stl}. The first emphasizes staged task logic
together with time-dependent connectivity requirements, whereas the
second highlights disjunctive region visitation under persistent
coupling. In both cases, the robustness traces agree with the formal STL
specifications, and the resulting trajectories demonstrate that the
combined use of batched robustness evaluation, sequential local
transport, and hybrid search yields feasible collaborative plans in
geometrically nontrivial environments.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch06_summary}

Chapter~\ref{chap:uncertainty} has examined how dynamic constraints and
environmental uncertainty reshape multi-robot planning at several
levels of abstraction.
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_safe_plan} addressed probabilistic temporal
tasks under safety and safe-return requirements through hierarchical
abstractions and policy synthesis.
Section~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_uncertain} then considered reactive
coordination under dynamic temporal tasks, emphasizing online adaptation
to moving targets and evolving task information.
Finally, Sec.~\ref{ch06:sec:ch06_stl} developed a continuous
trajectory-optimization perspective based on collaborative optimal
transport, in which STL robustness, geometric safety, and inter-robot
couplings are incorporated into a unified planning framework.
Taken together, these sections show that effective multi-robot planning
under uncertainty requires the coordinated use of symbolic abstraction,
reactive replanning, and continuous optimization, depending on whether
the dominant challenge lies in probabilistic task evolution, dynamic
task geometry, or tightly coupled motion synthesis.
