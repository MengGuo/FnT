\subsection{Case Study: Dual-team Exploration with Online Requests}
\label{sec:ch05_case_study}

This subsection illustrates the combined use of the latency-bounded
intermittent communication protocol in
Sec.~\ref{ch05:subsec:spread} and the topology-adaptive
request-fulfillment mechanism in Sec.~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_human}
through a representative dual-team exploration mission.

%==============================
\subsubsection{Workspace and task description}
\label{ch05:subsubsec:case_description}

Consider a dual-team exploration mission in an indoor office
environment of size \(90\,\mathrm{m}\times 70\,\mathrm{m}\). Each team
consists of four robots and one operator. In addition to the
exploration objective, four online service tasks are distributed in the
environment. These tasks include local inspection of target regions,
direct assistance to a robot, and remote tele-operation for opening an
access door. In accordance with the request model in
\eqref{ch05:eq:requests}, each operator may issue requests of types
\(\xi_2\), \(\xi_3\), and \(\xi_4\), together with migration commands,
whereas a robot that detects a service opportunity may trigger
\(\xi_5^i\) or \(\xi_6^i\). Both teams are initially deployed near the
top corridor of the map and begin exploration immediately after the
first communication round. The latency bounds for operator-fleet
communication and inter-team information exchange are set to
\(120\,\mathrm{s}\) and \(600\,\mathrm{s}\), respectively.

Figure~\ref{ch05:fig:case-overview} shows representative mission
snapshots together with the explored workspace. The figure includes the
priority regions induced by \(\xi_2\), the service-task locations, the
operator motions, and the explored areas of the two teams. In
particular, the region-priority request \(\xi_2\), implemented through
the frontier reweighting in \eqref{ch05:eq:frontier_weight}, biases the
local exploration process toward the designated areas while preserving
the latency-bounded wheel backbone from
Sec.~\ref{ch05:subsec:spread}. The mission further involves operator
relocation, chain-based service execution, and migration, thereby
activating the main realization modes introduced in
Sec.~\ref{ch05:subsubsec:request_modes}.

\subsubsection{Results}
\label{ch05:subsubsec:case_results}

The execution results are consistent with the analytical development in
Secs.~\ref{ch05:subsec:spread} and \ref{ch05:sec:ch05_human}. First,
the effect of the region-priority request is clearly visible in the
final coverage pattern: the neighborhoods of the designated priority
regions are explored only by the corresponding requesting team.
Moreover, because the two teams maintain different local frontier
sequences under intermittent communication, their feasible exploration
domains evolve differently, which in turn permits the two operators to
relocate in distinct directions. In the representative execution,
operator~1 completes three feasible relocations and eventually reaches
the lower part of the workspace, while operator~2 also completes three
feasible relocations and reaches the left portion of the map. These
relocations are consistent with the two realization modes of \(\xi_3\)
described in Sec.~\ref{ch05:subsubsec:request_modes}: whenever
\eqref{ch05:eq:base-location} is satisfied, only the embeddings are
updated; otherwise, a temporary migration topology is instantiated and
later merged back into the wheel backbone.

During the mission, team~1 detects and completes tasks~3 and~4, and
then executes a migration at \(t=1378\,\mathrm{s}\) toward the
lower-left area in order to continue exploration of the remaining free
space. Throughout this migration, all robots in team~1 remain connected
to operator~1. The remaining two tasks are detected and completed by
team~2, and all four online service tasks are finished by
\(t=1100\,\mathrm{s}\). Among them, one task is triggered by a
robot-initiated request, whereas the other three tasks are specified by
the two operators. To realize these service tasks, the method
instantiates communication-ensured chains according to
\eqref{ch05:eq:cec} with lengths \(2\) and \(3\), respectively. The
average computation time for generating a feasible chain is
approximately \(0.3\,\mathrm{s}\), and the average time for assigning
robots to the anchor points of the chain is about \(0.1\,\mathrm{s}\),
which is consistent with the lightweight role-assignment and topology
selection procedure in Algorithm~\ref{ch05:alg:topologymatch}.

The service durations depend on the task semantics once the chain has
been established. Inspection tasks require more sustained visual
interaction and therefore last between \(39\,\mathrm{s}\) and
\(152\,\mathrm{s}\), whereas the direct-assistance task and the door
operation last about \(25\,\mathrm{s}\). The exploration objective
continues after all online tasks have been fulfilled: operator~1
obtains the complete map at \(t=1624\,\mathrm{s}\), and operator~2
obtains the complete map at \(t=1782\,\mathrm{s}\). Hence the delays
between total task completion and full map completion are
\(524\,\mathrm{s}\) and \(682\,\mathrm{s}\), respectively. This
difference indicates that the online requests consume only part of the
mission time and that the exploration backbone continues to operate
effectively after the temporary request-specific subtopologies have been
removed.

A second observation concerns the inter-team information exchange. Over
the full execution, the two teams communicate with each other five
times, at \(504\,\mathrm{s}\), \(843\,\mathrm{s}\), \(1167\,\mathrm{s}\),
\(1594\,\mathrm{s}\), and \(2103\,\mathrm{s}\). The corresponding
inter-event gaps are \(339\,\mathrm{s}\), \(324\,\mathrm{s}\),
\(427\,\mathrm{s}\), and \(509\,\mathrm{s}\), so that the maximum
inter-team delay remains below the prescribed bound of
\(600\,\mathrm{s}\). The average inter-team exchange interval is
\(399.75\,\mathrm{s}\). Therefore, in this representative execution,
both the operator-fleet latency requirement and the imposed inter-team
communication bound are satisfied throughout the mission while online
requests continue to arrive and be executed.

Figure~\ref{ch05:fig:case-modes} shows the evolution of the operating
modes and the corresponding communication topologies. Both teams switch
among spread mode, chain mode, and migrate mode \(11\) times during the
execution. For team~1, the first switch occurs at \(446\,\mathrm{s}\),
when robot~4 detaches from the wheel backbone and becomes a messenger
node, while the remaining three robots preserve a smaller cyclic
structure and continue exploration. Later, in order to complete
task~3, the system undergoes a \(32\,\mathrm{s}\) transition to
construct a three-robot chain in chain mode. After task~3 has been
completed, the system switches directly to task~4 at
\(942\,\mathrm{s}\), so that the two tasks are served consecutively by
operator~1 with only \(21\,\mathrm{s}\) of switching time. This is
substantially shorter than the average \(38\,\mathrm{s}\) required for a
generic transition between spread mode and chain mode, corresponding to
a reduction of approximately \(44.7\%\). For team~2, chains of length
\(2\) are instantiated at \(275\,\mathrm{s}\) and
\(905\,\mathrm{s}\) to complete tasks~1 and~2. In these intervals,
operator~2 tele-operates the terminal robot through the chain while
performing local inspection around the task region. The experiment also
shows that the framework remains well-defined in degenerate cases where
the active communication topology in spread mode or chain mode contains
only one or two robots.

\caption{

Overall, the case study shows that the intermittent-communication
backbone maintains bounded-delay information propagation during
exploration, while the request-adaptive topology layer introduces only
temporary and localized reconfiguration when online service tasks
require additional structure. The results therefore demonstrate that
frontier-based exploration, operator relocation, chain-based
tele-operation, and direct robot assistance can be executed within a
common coordination architecture without sacrificing communication
feasibility.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch05_summary}


This chapter examined multi-robot coordination under partial knowledge
and limited communication, with emphasis on how communication
constraints should be treated as part of the planning problem rather
than as a passive execution condition. The chapter first considered the
joint design of communication and collaboration, where robot actions
and information exchange must be coordinated simultaneously in order to
maintain task feasibility. It then developed an exploration framework
under intermittent communication, in which the operator and the robot
fleet preserve bounded-delay information fusion through an embedded
joint-wheel communication topology while continuing frontier-based
exploration in an initially unknown workspace. On this basis, the
chapter introduced a topology-adaptive coordination layer for online
temporal requests, where the wheel backbone serves as the nominal
communication structure and is modified only when a request requires a
temporary reconfiguration of the embedded topology.

Taken together, the developments in
Sections~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_interm_team}--
\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_human} provide a unified view of multi-robot
coordination under sparse and delayed communication. Communication
maintenance, exploration, and request fulfillment are integrated within
a common planning and execution architecture, rather than treated as
separate modules. The resulting framework preserves latency-bounded
information flow whenever possible, supports online adaptation to newly
discovered workspace information and operator requests, and allows
temporary request-specific subtopologies to be instantiated and later
merged back into the wheel backbone. In this way,
Chapter~\ref{chap:communication} extends the earlier coordination
frameworks of Chapters~\ref{chap:bottom-up} and~\ref{chap:top-down} to
settings in which communication is intermittent, information is
incomplete, and topology adaptation is required for continued task
fulfillment.
