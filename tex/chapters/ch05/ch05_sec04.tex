\section{Case Study}
\label{sec:ch05_case_study}

This section presents two case studies to illustrate the methods
developed in Chapter~\ref{chap:communication}. The first case study is
associated with Section~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_interm_team} and examines
communication-aware coordination in a cluttered subterranean
environment under temporal and capability constraints. The second case
study combines the intermittent-communication exploration framework of
Section~\ref{ch05:sec:intermittent_comm} with the topology-adaptive online
request mechanism of Section~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_human} in a dual-team
exploration mission. Together, these studies provide execution-level
validation of the main formulations and coordination mechanisms of this
chapter under sparse communication, partial knowledge, and online task
updates.

%==============================
\subsection{Case study in a subterranean cave environment}
\label{ch05:subsec:case_subterranean_cave}

This first case study is associated with the communication-aware
coordination framework developed in
Section~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_interm_team}. In particular, it illustrates how
the workspace and robot model of
Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:env-task-model}, the online collaborative
temporal-task model of Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:task-spec}, the
communication constraints of Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:comm-const}, and
the adaptive coordination mechanism of
Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:coord-framework} are integrated within a
single execution scenario. The objective is to verify that the
proposed method can simultaneously maintain communication feasibility,
satisfy heterogeneous capability requirements, and enforce temporal
precedence constraints in a cluttered subterranean environment.

\subsubsection{Workspace and system description}
\label{ch05:subsubsec:case_subterranean_cave_workspace}

The simulation is conducted in a
$40 \times 70\,\mathrm{m}$ subterranean cave environment
containing irregular obstacles and narrow passages. The fleet
contains $10$ heterogeneous robots operating under the same
low-level motion and communication parameters, namely a
maximum speed of $v_i^{\max}=2\,\mathrm{m/s}$, a sensing
radius of $d_i=8\,\mathrm{m}$, and a communication range of
$r_i=10\,\mathrm{m}$. In this setting, the robots must not only
execute their assigned tasks, but also establish feasible
communication topologies around occlusions and bottlenecks so
that task allocation and subsequent coordination remain
well-defined in the presence of severe geometric constraints.

The mission contains four task classes with strict temporal
precedence constraints. Rescue tasks must be completed before
cargo-transportation tasks, and exploration tasks must be
completed before mineral-sampling tasks. These relations are
consistent with the temporal-constraint model in
\eqref{ch05:eq:task-constraints}. To realize the mission, the
fleet is divided into four capability classes: manipulation
robots for dexterous object handling, transportation robots for
payload delivery, obstacle-clearing robots for passage
preparation, and sensing robots for environmental perception.
The capability requirements are enforced throughout the
execution. Specifically, rescue tasks require one manipulation
robot and one transportation robot, cargo-transportation tasks
require one transportation robot and one obstacle-clearing
robot, exploration tasks require one sensing robot and one
obstacle-clearing robot, and mineral-sampling tasks require one
sensing robot and one manipulation robot. This construction
makes the scenario directly suitable for validating
Section~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_interm_team}, since both task
feasibility and communication feasibility must be satisfied
simultaneously.
The lower panel of
Figure~\ref{ch05:fig:case_subterranean_cave} records the local
timelines of all robots from $t=78\,\mathrm{s}$ to
$t=155\,\mathrm{s}$, while the upper panel reports
representative keyframes at $t=99\,\mathrm{s}$,
$t=106\,\mathrm{s}$, and $t=127\,\mathrm{s}$. The labels on
the left side of the Gantt chart identify the robots by
capability class and index; for example, \texttt{1\_1} denotes
the first robot in the first capability class. The chart also
distinguishes navigation, execution, communication, and idle
intervals, which provides a direct view of how the proposed
framework interleaves communication-graph formation with
task execution.

\subsubsection{Execution results}
\label{ch05:subsubsec:case_subterranean_cave_results}

The execution results confirm that the proposed framework
produces a feasible and logically consistent joint plan under
the model of Section~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_interm_team}. The
observed communication reorganizations at $t=99\,\mathrm{s}$
and $t=127\,\mathrm{s}$ are consistent with the team-wise
connectivity condition in \eqref{ch05:eq:comm-graph} and with
the communication-event optimization mechanism of
Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:comm-event}, especially the
optimization model in \eqref{ch05:eq:comm-opt}. Moreover, the
task timeline respects the temporal-relation constraints in
\eqref{ch05:eq:task-constraints}, while the resulting task
dispatch is consistent with the assignment step in
\eqref{ch05:eq:task-assign}. The sparse use of communication
events is also aligned with the adaptive planning objective in
\eqref{ch05:eq:adaptive_objective}. Hence, this case study
serves as an execution-level validation of the formulation in
Section~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_interm_team}, rather than merely a
qualitative illustration of robot behavior.

Starting from $t=78\,\mathrm{s}$, the team first establishes a
communication graph at $t=99\,\mathrm{s}$, that is, after an
initial deployment period of $21\,\mathrm{s}$. A representative
execution state is observed at $t=106\,\mathrm{s}$, only
$7\,\mathrm{s}$ after the first communication phase, where two
robot teams are already executing assigned tasks while the
remaining robots continue navigating toward their designated
goal locations. A second communication reorganization takes
place at $t=127\,\mathrm{s}$, and the overall mission is
completed at $t=155\,\mathrm{s}$. Hence, the full execution
horizon is $77\,\mathrm{s}$, and only two communication phases
are required over the entire run, corresponding to one
communication-driven reorganization every
$38.5\,\mathrm{s}$ on average.
The temporal logic of the mission is respected throughout the
execution. In particular, the snapshot at $t=118\,\mathrm{s}$
shows that, although the designated robots for exploration and
mineral sampling have both reached the corresponding target
regions, the exploration task is executed first, and the
mineral-sampling task is deferred until the prescribed
precedence condition is satisfied. The capability constraints
are also fulfilled consistently. Rescue tasks are executed only
by teams consisting of manipulation and transportation robots,
cargo-transportation tasks by teams consisting of
transportation and obstacle-clearing robots, exploration tasks
by teams consisting of sensing and obstacle-clearing robots,
and mineral-sampling tasks by teams consisting of sensing and
manipulation robots. No incompatible robot--task assignment
appears in the execution timeline.

From the mission-level perspective, the system completes
$16$ tasks within the $77\,\mathrm{s}$ horizon from
$t=78\,\mathrm{s}$ to $t=155\,\mathrm{s}$. This corresponds to
an average throughput of approximately
$16/77 \approx 0.208$ tasks per second, or equivalently about
$4.81\,\mathrm{s}$ per completed task when normalized by the
full mission duration. Since the fleet contains $10$ robots,
the completed workload corresponds to an average of
$1.6$ accomplished tasks per robot over the trial. Moreover,
the interval from the first communication phase at
$t=99\,\mathrm{s}$ to mission completion is only
$56\,\mathrm{s}$, and the second communication phase occurs
exactly $28\,\mathrm{s}$ after the first and $28\,\mathrm{s}$
before mission completion. This temporal structure indicates
that the framework does not rely on frequent global
reorganization; rather, it uses sparse communication events to
update the task allocation and then sustains execution through
local motion and coordination.
The Gantt chart further shows that most robots spend the
majority of their active time in navigation rather than idle
waiting, while the communication intervals remain localized
around the two designated reorganization stages. This behavior
is consistent with the online execution mechanism in
Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:online}: communication is invoked when
needed to synchronize allocation and maintain team-level
consistency, but it does not dominate the mission timeline.
Overall, these results demonstrate that the proposed
framework can coordinate a heterogeneous multi-robot team in a
cluttered subterranean environment while simultaneously
handling communication constraints, temporal precedence
constraints, and capability-matching requirements.

\subsection{Case study of dual-team exploration with online tasks}
\label{ch05:subsec:case_dual_team_exploration}

This second case study illustrates the combined use of the
latency-bounded intermittent-communication framework in
Section~\ref{ch05:sec:intermittent_comm} and the topology-adaptive
request-fulfillment mechanism in Section~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_human}
through a representative dual-team exploration mission. The
objective is to show that the wheel-based exploration backbone
can continue to satisfy bounded-delay information fusion while
temporarily reconfiguring its local topology to accommodate
online requests issued by operators or triggered by robots
during execution.

%==============================
\subsubsection{Workspace and task description}
\label{ch05:subsubsec:case_description}

Consider a dual-team exploration mission in an indoor office
environment of size \(90\,\mathrm{m}\times 70\,\mathrm{m}\). Each team
consists of four robots and one operator. In addition to the
exploration objective, four online service tasks are distributed in the
environment. These tasks include local inspection of target regions,
direct assistance to a robot, and remote teleoperation for opening an
access door. In accordance with the request model in
\eqref{ch05:eq:requests}, each operator may issue requests of types
\(\xi_2\), \(\xi_3\), and \(\xi_4\), together with migration commands,
whereas a robot that detects a service opportunity may trigger
\(\xi_5^i\) or \(\xi_6^i\). Both teams are initially deployed near the
top corridor of the map and begin exploration immediately after the
first communication round. The latency bounds for operator-fleet
communication and inter-team information exchange are set to
\(120\,\mathrm{s}\) and \(600\,\mathrm{s}\), respectively.
Figure~\ref{ch05:fig:case-overview} shows representative mission
snapshots together with the explored workspace. The figure includes the
priority regions induced by \(\xi_2\), the service-task locations, the
operator motions, and the explored areas of the two teams. In
particular, the region-priority request \(\xi_2\), implemented through
the frontier reweighting in \eqref{ch05:eq:frontier_weight}, biases the
local exploration process toward the designated areas while preserving
the latency-bounded wheel backbone from
Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:spread}. The mission further involves operator
relocation, chain-based service execution, and migration, thereby
activating the main realization modes introduced in
Section~\ref{ch05:subsec:requests} and
Subsubsec.~\ref{ch05:subsubsec:request_modes}.

\subsubsection{Exploration and online execution results}
\label{ch05:subsubsec:case_results}

The execution results are consistent with the analytical development in
Sections~\ref{ch05:sec:intermittent_comm} and~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_human}.
First, the effect of the region-priority request is clearly visible in
the final coverage pattern: the neighborhoods of the designated
priority regions are explored only by the corresponding requesting
team. Moreover, because the two teams maintain different local frontier
sequences under intermittent communication, their feasible exploration
domains evolve differently, which in turn permits the two operators to
relocate in distinct directions. In the representative execution,
operator~1 completes three feasible relocations and eventually reaches
the lower part of the workspace, while operator~2 also completes three
feasible relocations and reaches the left portion of the map. These
relocations are consistent with the two realization modes of \(\xi_3\)
described in Subsubsec.~\ref{ch05:subsubsec:request_modes}: whenever
\eqref{ch05:eq:base-location} is satisfied, only the embeddings are
updated; otherwise, a temporary migration topology is instantiated and
later merged back into the wheel backbone.

During the mission, team~1 detects and completes tasks~3 and~4, and
then executes a migration at \(t=1378\,\mathrm{s}\) toward the
lower-left area in order to continue exploration of the remaining free
space. Throughout this migration, all robots in team~1 remain connected
to operator~1. The remaining two tasks are detected and completed by
team~2, and all four online service tasks are finished by
\(t=1100\,\mathrm{s}\). Among them, one task is triggered by a
robot-initiated request, whereas the other three tasks are specified by
the two operators. To realize these service tasks, the method
instantiates communication-ensured chains according to
\eqref{ch05:eq:cec} with lengths \(2\) and \(3\), respectively. The
average computation time for generating a feasible chain is
approximately \(0.3\,\mathrm{s}\), and the average time for assigning
robots to the anchor points of the chain is about \(0.1\,\mathrm{s}\),
which is consistent with the lightweight role-assignment and topology
selection procedure in Algorithm~\ref{ch05:alg:topologymatch}. The
service durations depend on the task semantics once the chain has been
established. Inspection tasks require more sustained visual interaction
and therefore last between \(39\,\mathrm{s}\) and \(152\,\mathrm{s}\),
whereas the direct-assistance task and the door operation last about
\(25\,\mathrm{s}\). The exploration objective continues after all
online tasks have been fulfilled: operator~1 obtains the complete map
at \(t=1624\,\mathrm{s}\), and operator~2 obtains the complete map at
\(t=1782\,\mathrm{s}\). Hence the delays between total task completion
and full map completion are \(524\,\mathrm{s}\) and \(682\,\mathrm{s}\),
respectively. This difference indicates that the online requests
consume only part of the mission time and that the exploration backbone
continues to operate effectively after the temporary request-specific
subtopologies have been removed.

A second observation concerns the inter-team information exchange. Over
the full execution, the two teams communicate with each other five
times, at \(504\,\mathrm{s}\), \(843\,\mathrm{s}\),
\(1167\,\mathrm{s}\), \(1594\,\mathrm{s}\), and \(2103\,\mathrm{s}\).
The corresponding inter-event gaps are \(339\,\mathrm{s}\),
\(324\,\mathrm{s}\), \(427\,\mathrm{s}\), and \(509\,\mathrm{s}\), so
that the maximum inter-team delay remains below the prescribed bound of
\(600\,\mathrm{s}\). The average inter-team exchange interval is
\(399.75\,\mathrm{s}\). Therefore, in this representative execution,
both the operator-fleet latency requirement and the imposed inter-team
communication bound are satisfied throughout the mission while online
requests continue to arrive and be executed.
Figure~\ref{ch05:fig:case-modes} shows the evolution of the operating
modes and the corresponding communication topologies. The two teams
undergo \(11\) mode switches in total among spread mode, chain mode,
and migrate mode during execution. For team~1, the first switch occurs
at \(446\,\mathrm{s}\), when robot~4 detaches from the wheel backbone
and becomes a messenger node, while the remaining three robots
preserve a smaller cyclic structure and continue exploration. Later, in
order to complete task~3, the system undergoes a \(32\,\mathrm{s}\)
transition to construct a three-robot chain in chain mode. After
task~3 has been completed, the system switches directly to task~4 at
\(942\,\mathrm{s}\), so that the two tasks are served consecutively by
operator~1 with only \(21\,\mathrm{s}\) of switching time. This is
substantially shorter than the average \(38\,\mathrm{s}\) required for
a generic transition between spread mode and chain mode, corresponding
to a reduction of approximately \(44.7\%\). For team~2, chains of
length \(2\) are instantiated at \(275\,\mathrm{s}\) and
\(905\,\mathrm{s}\) to complete tasks~1 and~2. In these intervals,
operator~2 teleoperates the terminal robot through the chain while
performing local inspection around the task region. The experiment also
shows that the framework remains well-defined in degenerate cases where
the active communication topology in spread mode or chain mode contains
only one or two robots.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch05_summary}

This chapter studied multi-robot coordination under partial knowledge
and limited communication. It first considered communication-aware
collaboration, where robot actions and information exchange are planned
jointly to maintain task feasibility. It then developed an
intermittent-communication exploration framework based on an embedded
joint-wheel topology for bounded-delay information fusion in unknown
workspaces. Building on this framework, the chapter introduced a
topology-adaptive coordination layer for online temporal requests.
Collectively, the developments in
Sections~\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_interm_team}--
\ref{ch05:sec:ch05_human} provide a unified framework for multi-robot
coordination under sparse and delayed communication. In this way,
Chapter~\ref{chap:communication} extends the earlier coordination
frameworks of Chapters~\ref{chap:bottom-up} and~\ref{chap:top-down} to
settings with intermittent communication, incomplete information, and
topology adaptation.
