\section{Task Relaxation and Adaptation in Partially Known Workspaces}
\label{sec:task_relaxation_adaptation}

The synthesis framework in Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries} presumes that the
weighted transition system is available before execution and remains fixed.
This assumption is restrictive in partially known workspaces, where blocked
passages, newly discovered traversable regions, or revised semantic labels may
appear only after the robot starts moving. Under such uncertainty, a local LTL
specification may be infeasible over the current abstraction even when the
physical task is achievable in the actual workspace. At the same time,
safety-critical requirements must remain enforceable throughout execution.

This section addresses that setting for a single robot. The local task is split
into a hard component, which must always be satisfied, and a soft component,
which should be satisfied to the largest possible extent. The first part of the
section develops a relaxed synthesis mechanism for the case in which exact
satisfaction is not currently available. The second part introduces a
history-aware adaptation rule that revises the remaining plan whenever the
workspace abstraction is updated. The construction is related to specification
relaxation and automata-based replanning
\cite{kim2012approximate,guo2013revising,livingston2012backtracking}, but is
presented here in notation consistent with
Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries}.

\subsection{Problem Setting}
\label{subsec:problem_setting_relaxation}

Let the initial abstraction of the workspace be denoted by
$\mathcal{T}^{0} \triangleq
(S,S_{0},\rightarrow^{0},\mathsf{AP},L^{0},w^{0})$, where the components
follow \eqref{eq:transition-system}. During execution, newly acquired
information induces a family of updated abstractions
$\{\mathcal{T}^{t}\}_{t\geq 0}$ with the same state set $S$ and alphabet
$\mathsf{AP}$, but possibly revised transition relation, labeling function,
and transition weights.

The local task is decomposed as
\begin{equation}
\varphi \triangleq
\varphi^{\mathrm{soft}} \wedge \varphi^{\mathrm{hard}},
\label{eq:task_split_partial}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi^{\mathrm{hard}}$ collects safety and mission-preserving
requirements that may not be violated, and
$\varphi^{\mathrm{soft}}$ collects liveness or performance goals that may be
temporarily unattainable over the current model. The planning problem is thus
not only to determine whether \eqref{eq:task_split_partial} is feasible over
$\mathcal{T}^{0}$, but also to synthesize a run that always satisfies the hard
component and minimizes the violation of the soft component whenever exact
satisfaction is unavailable.

Let $\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{s}} \triangleq
(Q_{\mathrm{s}},Q_{\mathrm{s},0},2^{\mathsf{AP}},
\delta_{\mathrm{s}},F_{\mathrm{s}})$ and
$\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{h}} \triangleq
(Q_{\mathrm{h}},Q_{\mathrm{h},0},2^{\mathsf{AP}},
\delta_{\mathrm{h}},F_{\mathrm{h}})$ be B\"uchi automata for
$\varphi^{\mathrm{soft}}$ and $\varphi^{\mathrm{hard}}$, respectively
\cite{baier2008principles,vardi1986automata,gastin2001fast}. The symbols
$\rho$, $\varrho$, and the projection notation are used exactly as in
Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries}: $\rho$ denotes a run of the transition
system, while $\varrho$ denotes a run of a product automaton.

\subsection{Relaxed Intersection of Hard and Soft Tasks}
\label{subsec:relaxed_intersection}

The task split in \eqref{eq:task_split_partial} suggests that the hard and soft
components should be treated asymmetrically. The hard component must be checked
exactly, whereas the soft component may be advanced even when its transition
guard is not perfectly matched by the current workspace label. This asymmetry is
captured by a relaxed intersection automaton.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:relaxed_intersection}
The \emph{relaxed intersection automaton} associated with
$\varphi^{\mathrm{soft}}$ and $\varphi^{\mathrm{hard}}$ is the B\"uchi
automaton
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\mathcal{B}}_{\varphi} \triangleq
\bigl(\widetilde{Q},\widetilde{Q}_{0},2^{\mathsf{AP}},
\widetilde{\delta},\widetilde{F}\bigr),
\label{eq:relaxed_intersection_automaton}
\end{equation}
where
$\widetilde{Q} \triangleq
Q_{\mathrm{s}} \times Q_{\mathrm{h}} \times \{1,2\}$,
$\widetilde{Q}_{0} \triangleq
Q_{\mathrm{s},0} \times Q_{\mathrm{h},0} \times \{1\}$, and
\(\widetilde{F} \triangleq
(Q_{\mathrm{s}} \times F_{\mathrm{h}} \times \{1\})
\cup (F_{\mathrm{s}} \times Q_{\mathrm{h}} \times \{2\})\).
For any label $\ell \in 2^{\mathsf{AP}}$, a transition from
$\widetilde{q}=(q_{\mathrm{s}},q_{\mathrm{h}},\iota)$ to
$\widetilde{q}'=(q'_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{h}},\iota')$ belongs to
$\widetilde{\delta}(\widetilde{q},\ell)$ if and only if three conditions are
satisfied. First, the hard component advances exactly, namely
$q'_{\mathrm{h}} \in \delta_{\mathrm{h}}(q_{\mathrm{h}},\ell)$. Second, the
soft successor is admissible in the sense that there exists at least one label
$\ell' \in 2^{\mathsf{AP}}$ such that
$q'_{\mathrm{s}} \in \delta_{\mathrm{s}}(q_{\mathrm{s}},\ell')$. Third, the
phase variable updates according to the alternating acceptance discipline:
$\iota' = 2$ if $\iota = 1$ and $q'_{\mathrm{h}} \in F_{\mathrm{h}}$,
$\iota' = 1$ if $\iota = 2$ and $q'_{\mathrm{s}} \in F_{\mathrm{s}}$, and
$\iota' = \iota$ otherwise.
\end{definition}

Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_intersection} preserves the hard component exactly,
since every transition must satisfy the hard guard under the current label.
Only the soft component is relaxed, and even there the relaxation is limited to
transitions that are logically available in the soft automaton. The resulting
construction therefore enlarges the feasible search space only along the soft
dimension, while retaining the exact safety structure of the hard component.

For any two soft-automaton states $q,q' \in Q_{\mathrm{s}}$, define the
admissible soft label set by
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{s}}(q,q') \triangleq
\{\ell \in 2^{\mathsf{AP}} \mid q' \in \delta_{\mathrm{s}}(q,\ell)\}$.
For a current workspace label $\ell \in 2^{\mathsf{AP}}$, define the mismatch
measure
\begin{equation}
d_{\mathrm{s}}(\ell,q,q') \triangleq
\min_{\ell' \in \Lambda_{\mathrm{s}}(q,q')} |\ell \triangle \ell'|,
\label{eq:soft_distance}
\end{equation}
where $\triangle$ denotes symmetric difference.
Equation~\eqref{eq:soft_distance} is well defined whenever
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{s}}(q,q') \neq \varnothing$, which is already enforced in
Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_intersection}. The value of
\eqref{eq:soft_distance} is zero if and only if the current label enables the
soft transition exactly. Accordingly, the transition relation in
\eqref{eq:relaxed_intersection_automaton} can be computed by first enumerating
all hard transitions enabled by the current label and then, for each admissible
soft successor, assigning the mismatch value from
\eqref{eq:soft_distance}. The phase update is purely combinatorial and does not
introduce any additional optimization variable.

The resulting computation is finite because both B\"uchi automata and the
alphabet are finite. It is also modular: the hard transition relation, the soft
admissible label sets, and the phase update rule can all be precomputed before
the relaxed product is constructed. This separation becomes useful when the
workspace model changes on-line, because the automata-side preprocessing does
not need to be repeated.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Construction of the relaxed intersection automaton}
\label{alg:relaxed_intersection}
\DontPrintSemicolon
\KwIn{$\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{s}}$, $\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{h}}$}
\KwOut{$\widetilde{\mathcal{B}}_{\varphi}$ and mismatch table
$ d_{\mathrm{s}}(\ell,q,q') $}
Initialize $\widetilde{Q}$, $\widetilde{Q}_{0}$, and $\widetilde{F}$ according
to \eqref{eq:relaxed_intersection_automaton}\;
Set $\widetilde{\delta} \leftarrow \varnothing$\;
\ForEach{$(q_{\mathrm{s}},q_{\mathrm{h}},\iota) \in \widetilde{Q}$}{
  \ForEach{$\ell \in 2^{\mathsf{AP}}$}{
    Compute the enabled hard successors
    $\delta_{\mathrm{h}}(q_{\mathrm{h}},\ell)$\;
    \ForEach{$q'_{\mathrm{h}} \in \delta_{\mathrm{h}}(q_{\mathrm{h}},\ell)$}{
      \ForEach{$q'_{\mathrm{s}} \in Q_{\mathrm{s}}$}{
        Compute
        $\Lambda_{\mathrm{s}}(q_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{s}})$\;
        \If{$\Lambda_{\mathrm{s}}(q_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{s}})
        \neq \varnothing$}{
          Compute
          $d_{\mathrm{s}}(\ell,q_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{s}})$ by
          \eqref{eq:soft_distance}\;
          Update the phase variable $\iota'$ by
          Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_intersection}\;
          Insert the transition
          $(q_{\mathrm{s}},q_{\mathrm{h}},\iota)
          \xrightarrow{\ell}
          (q'_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{h}},\iota')$ into
          $\widetilde{\delta}$\;
        }
      }
    }
  }
}
\Return{$\widetilde{\mathcal{B}}_{\varphi}$ and the mismatch table}\;
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:relaxed_intersection} makes the computational role of the
relaxed intersection explicit. The output is not only the automaton
$\widetilde{\mathcal{B}}_{\varphi}$ itself, but also the mismatch table from
\eqref{eq:soft_distance}, which will later enter the product-edge weights. In
this sense, the relaxed intersection construction serves simultaneously as a
logical relaxation mechanism and as a preprocessing stage for quantitative
planning.

The accepting condition in
\eqref{eq:relaxed_intersection_automaton} alternates between the hard and soft
accepting sets. As a consequence, every accepting run of
$\widetilde{\mathcal{B}}_{\varphi}$ induces infinitely many visits to the hard
accepting set while maintaining synchronization with the soft component.
Hard-task correctness is stated formally after the relaxed product is
introduced, since that property concerns the product run and its projection onto
$\mathcal{T}^{t}$.

\subsection{Relaxed Product and Balanced Synthesis}
\label{subsec:relaxed_product_balanced}

The relaxed intersection automaton must now be coupled with the robot model.
This yields a product construction that preserves the transition-system
semantics from Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries}, while attaching an additional
penalty to each product edge whenever the soft transition is not exactly matched
by the current workspace label.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:relaxed_product}
For any workspace abstraction
$\mathcal{T}^{t}=(S,S_{0},\rightarrow^{t},\mathsf{AP},L^{t},w^{t})$, the
\emph{relaxed product automaton} is defined by
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t} \triangleq
\mathcal{T}^{t} \otimes \widetilde{\mathcal{B}}_{\varphi}
=
\bigl(\widetilde{X}^{t},\widetilde{X}_{0}^{t},
\rightarrow_{\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t}},
\widetilde{F}^{t},\widetilde{w}^{t}\bigr),
\label{eq:relaxed_product_automaton}
\end{equation}
where
$\widetilde{X}^{t} \triangleq S \times \widetilde{Q}$,
$\widetilde{X}_{0}^{t} \triangleq S_{0} \times \widetilde{Q}_{0}$, and
$\widetilde{F}^{t} \triangleq S \times \widetilde{F}$.
A product transition from
$x=(s,q_{\mathrm{s}},q_{\mathrm{h}},\iota)$ to
$x'=(s',q'_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{h}},\iota')$ belongs to
$\rightarrow_{\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t}}$ if and only if
$s \rightarrow^{t} s'$ and
$(q'_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{h}},\iota')
\in \widetilde{\delta}((q_{\mathrm{s}},q_{\mathrm{h}},\iota),L^{t}(s))$.
\end{definition}

Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_product} differs from the standard product in
Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries} only through the use of the relaxed
intersection automaton. The state projection of every run of
$\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t}$ is therefore a valid run of $\mathcal{T}^{t}$.
The distinction between exact and relaxed planning is thus encoded entirely in
the automaton component and in the edge weights, not in the semantics of the
transition-system projection.

For each enabled product transition in
\eqref{eq:relaxed_product_automaton}, define its weight by
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{w}^{t}(x,x') \triangleq
w^{t}(s,s') + \alpha \,
 d_{\mathrm{s}}\bigl(L^{t}(s),q_{\mathrm{s}},q'_{\mathrm{s}}\bigr),
\label{eq:relaxed_product_weight}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \geq 0$ is a design parameter.
Equation~\eqref{eq:relaxed_product_weight} separates implementation cost from
soft-task mismatch. The first term is inherited from the weighted transition
system. The second term is zero when the soft transition is exactly enabled, and
positive otherwise. The parameter $\alpha$ therefore adjusts the tradeoff
between motion efficiency and tolerance for temporary soft-task violation.

As in Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries}, accepting runs are considered in
prefix--suffix form. For
$\varrho=x_{0}x_{1}\cdots x_{K}(x_{K+1}\cdots x_{N}x_{K})^{\omega}$ with
$x_{0} \in \widetilde{X}_{0}^{t}$ and $x_{K} \in \widetilde{F}^{t}$, define
\begin{equation}
J_{\mathrm{bal}}^{t}(\varrho) \triangleq
\Bigl(\sum_{k=0}^{K-1} + \gamma \sum_{k=K}^{N}\Bigr)
\widetilde{w}^{t}(x_{k},x_{k+1}),
\label{eq:balanced_cost_relaxed}
\end{equation}
where $x_{N+1} \triangleq x_{K}$ and $\gamma \geq 0$ weights the suffix
relative to the prefix. By substituting
\eqref{eq:relaxed_product_weight} into
\eqref{eq:balanced_cost_relaxed}, the total cost decomposes into a motion term
and a cumulative soft-violation term. The search problem on the relaxed product
therefore remains structurally identical to the one in
Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries}, while its objective now reflects both motion
quality and logical relaxation.

The computation proceeds in three steps. First,
$\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t}$ is constructed by
Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_product}. Second, every enabled edge is assigned a
weight through \eqref{eq:relaxed_product_weight}. Third, the standard
accepting-run search over a weighted product graph is applied with objective
\eqref{eq:balanced_cost_relaxed}
\cite{smith2011optimal,schuppan2005shortest}. The resulting minimizer is
denoted by $\varrho^{t,\star}$, and the associated motion plan is
$\rho^{t,\star} \triangleq \mathrm{proj}_{S}(\varrho^{t,\star})$.

The next result states the structural safety guarantee of the relaxation.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:hard_task_preservation}
Let $\varrho$ be an accepting run of $\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t}$ and let
$\rho \triangleq \mathrm{proj}_{S}(\varrho)$. Then $\rho$ is a valid run of
$\mathcal{T}^{t}$ and $\rho \models \varphi^{\mathrm{hard}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Validity follows from Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_product}, since every product
edge projects to an enabled transition of $\mathcal{T}^{t}$. For the hard task,
Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_intersection} requires every product edge to follow
an actual transition of $\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{h}}$ under the current label.
Hence the trace of $\rho$ induces a run of $\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{h}}$.
Because the phase variable in
\eqref{eq:relaxed_intersection_automaton} enforces infinitely many visits to
$F_{\mathrm{h}}$, that induced run is accepting. Therefore
$\rho \models \varphi^{\mathrm{hard}}$.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:hard_task_preservation} shows that the relaxation never acts
on the hard component. The next lemma identifies the case in which the relaxed
solution recovers exact satisfaction of the full task.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:zero_soft_violation}
Let $\varrho^{t,\star}$ be a minimizer of
\eqref{eq:balanced_cost_relaxed}, and let
$\rho^{t,\star} \triangleq \mathrm{proj}_{S}(\varrho^{t,\star})$.
If every edge of $\varrho^{t,\star}$ has zero mismatch in
\eqref{eq:relaxed_product_weight}, then
$\rho^{t,\star} \models
\varphi^{\mathrm{soft}} \wedge \varphi^{\mathrm{hard}}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Zero mismatch on every edge means that each traversed soft transition is enabled
by the actual workspace label. Consequently, the trace of $\rho^{t,\star}$
induces an accepting run of $\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{s}}$. The hard part follows
from Theorem~\ref{thm:hard_task_preservation}. Therefore the complete formula in
\eqref{eq:task_split_partial} is satisfied.
\end{proof}

Lemma~\ref{lem:zero_soft_violation} confirms that the relaxed construction is a
strict extension of the exact one. Whenever the current abstraction already
supports exact satisfaction, the mismatch term in
\eqref{eq:relaxed_product_weight} vanishes along the optimal run. The relaxed
product should therefore be viewed as a conservative extension of the exact
product rather than as a different synthesis problem.

\subsection{On-line Plan Adaptation with History Consistency}
\label{subsec:online_update_and_revision}

The initial plan is synthesized on $\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{0}$. During
execution, new information may update the abstraction from
$\mathcal{T}^{t^-}$ to $\mathcal{T}^{t^+}$. The current plan must then be
validated against the updated model before execution proceeds. This validation
step is necessary because a previously feasible path may become blocked, and a
previously admissible label may cease to support the hard-automaton transition
required by the current product run.

Let $\varrho^{t}$ be the product run currently selected for execution, and let
$\rho^{\mathrm{past}}_{t}$ be the finite portion of its state projection already
executed when the update occurs. An edge of $\varrho^{t}$ is called
\emph{invalid} if its state projection is not a transition of
$\mathcal{T}^{t^+}$. It is called \emph{unsafe} if the updated source label no
longer enables its hard-automaton transition. If no edge in the unexecuted part
of $\varrho^{t}$ is invalid or unsafe, the current plan remains admissible.
Otherwise, replanning is necessary. This distinction between invalidity and
unsafety is useful because the first concerns motion feasibility, whereas the
second concerns preservation of the hard temporal requirement.

Since the B\"uchi components may be nondeterministic, the current workspace
state alone is insufficient to determine the current product state. The
executed history must therefore be retained. Define the set of
history-consistent product states by
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{X}^{t}_{\mathrm{past}} \triangleq
\Bigl\{x \in \widetilde{X}^{t^+} \, \Big| \,
\exists\text{ a finite run of } \widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t^+}
\text{ ending at } x
\text{ whose state projection is }
\rho^{\mathrm{past}}_{t}\Bigr\},
\label{eq:history_consistent_states}
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{X}^{t^+}$ denotes the state set of the updated relaxed
product. Equation~\eqref{eq:history_consistent_states} is the consistency
constraint that links the future plan to the executed trace. It guarantees that
replanning resumes from a product state compatible with the history already
realized by the robot, rather than from an arbitrary state sharing only the same
workspace position.

The computation after an update is therefore conceptually simple, although it
must be carried out at the product level. The remaining part of the current plan
is checked for invalid or unsafe edges. If none are found, the current plan is
retained. Otherwise, the updated relaxed product is rebuilt, the initial state
set is replaced by the history-consistent set in
\eqref{eq:history_consistent_states}, and the balanced synthesis problem in
\eqref{eq:balanced_cost_relaxed} is solved again. The new accepting suffix is
then concatenated with the already executed prefix through the corresponding
product-state history.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{History-aware revision under workspace updates}
\label{alg:history_revision}
\DontPrintSemicolon
\KwIn{$\mathcal{T}^{t^+}$, current run $\varrho^{t}$, executed prefix
$\rho^{\mathrm{past}}_{t}$}
\KwOut{Updated accepting run}
Inspect the unexecuted part of $\varrho^{t}$ on $\mathcal{T}^{t^+}$\;
\If{no remaining edge is invalid or unsafe}{
  \Return{$\varrho^{t}$}\;
}
Construct $\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t^+}$ by
Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_product}\;
Compute the history-consistent state set
$\widetilde{X}^{t}_{\mathrm{past}}$ by
\eqref{eq:history_consistent_states}\;
Replace the initial state set of $\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t^+}$ by
$\widetilde{X}^{t}_{\mathrm{past}}$\;
Assign all edge weights by \eqref{eq:relaxed_product_weight}\;
Solve the accepting-run search with objective
\eqref{eq:balanced_cost_relaxed}\;
Let the optimizer be $\varrho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t}$\;
\Return{$\varrho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t}$}\;
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:history_revision} emphasizes that replanning is triggered
only when the previously synthesized continuation is no longer admissible under
the updated abstraction. In particular, the method does not discard a valid plan
merely because a potentially better one might exist after the update. This
choice reduces unnecessary recomputation and preserves consistency with the
executed trace whenever the original continuation remains sound.

The following statement formalizes the correctness of the revision step.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:history_aware_revision}
Let $\varrho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t}$ be an accepting run of
$\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t^+}$ whose initial state belongs to
$\widetilde{X}^{t}_{\mathrm{past}}$, and let
$\rho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t} \triangleq
\mathrm{proj}_{S}(\varrho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t})$.
Then the concatenated trajectory
$\rho^{\mathrm{comp}}_{t} \triangleq
\rho^{\mathrm{past}}_{t} + \rho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t}$ is a valid run of
$\mathcal{T}^{t^+}$ and satisfies $\varphi^{\mathrm{hard}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \eqref{eq:history_consistent_states}, the initial state of
$\varrho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t}$ is reachable in
$\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}^{t^+}$ through a finite run whose state projection is
exactly $\rho^{\mathrm{past}}_{t}$. Concatenating that finite run with
$\varrho^{\mathrm{new}}_{t}$ yields an accepting run of the updated relaxed
product whose state projection is $\rho^{\mathrm{comp}}_{t}$. The validity of
$\rho^{\mathrm{comp}}_{t}$ follows from
Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_product}, and hard-task satisfaction follows from
Theorem~\ref{thm:hard_task_preservation}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:history_aware_revision} clarifies the role of history in
partially known workspaces. The revision mechanism does not merely reconnect the
current workspace state to a new goal. Instead, it reconstructs a product-level
continuation that is compatible with the trace already produced by the robot,
while preserving the hard component of the original specification and improving
soft-task satisfaction whenever the updated model permits it.

\subsection{Task-Driven Collaborative Knowledge Transfer}
\label{subsec:task_driven_transfer}

The single-robot mechanism in
Subsection~\ref{subsec:online_update_and_revision} extends naturally to a team
of robots whose local tasks are independent, but whose workspace knowledge is
not. Consider a finite index set
$\mathcal{N} \triangleq \{1,\ldots,N\}$. For each robot $i \in \mathcal{N}$,
let
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}_{i}^{t} \triangleq
\bigl(S_{i},S_{i,0},\rightarrow_{i}^{t},\mathsf{AP}_{i},L_{i}^{t},w_{i}^{t}
\bigr),
\label{eq:local_team_ts}
\end{equation}
where the components follow the notation of
Chapter~\ref{chap:preliminaries}. The local task of robot $i$ is
$\varphi_{i} \triangleq
\varphi_{i}^{\mathrm{soft}} \wedge \varphi_{i}^{\mathrm{hard}}$, and the
associated relaxed product automaton
$\widetilde{\mathcal{P}}_{i}^{t}$ is constructed exactly as in
Definition~\ref{def:relaxed_product}. The independence assumption means that
no synchronous team product is introduced: each robot plans over its own
abstraction, while communication is used only to improve the information on
which that abstraction is built. Related distributed viewpoints appear in
\cite{durfee2006distributed,filippidis2012decentralized,guo2014distributed}.

The communication layer is modeled by a time-varying directed graph
$\mathcal{G}^{t} \triangleq (\mathcal{N},\mathcal{E}^{t})$, where
$(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}^{t}$ means that robot $i$ can transmit to robot $j$ at
time $t$. Denote by $\mathcal{N}_{i}^{t}$ the outgoing neighbors of robot $i$.
For each robot $i$, define the set of task-relevant propositions by
$\mathsf{AP}_{i}^{\varphi} \triangleq
\{\alpha \in \mathsf{AP}_{i} \mid \alpha \text{ appears in } \varphi_{i}\}$.
Only propositions in $\mathsf{AP}_{i}^{\varphi}$ can influence the automata
for $\varphi_{i}$ and, consequently, only those propositions are worth
requesting from other robots. This observation motivates a task-driven
subscriber--publisher protocol in which each robot advertises only its
task-relevant proposition set and each outgoing reply is filtered accordingly.

Suppose robot $i$ acquires at time $t$ a sensing packet
$\mathbf{Sense}_{i}^{t}$ consisting of tuples
$(s,\Sigma^{+},\Sigma^{-})$, where
$s \in S_{i}$ and $\Sigma^{+},\Sigma^{-} \subseteq \mathsf{AP}_{i}$ denote,
respectively, propositions newly confirmed and newly excluded at state $s$.
If robot $j$ has previously subscribed to robot $i$ by sending the request
$\mathbf{Req}_{j \rightarrow i} \triangleq (j,\mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi})$,
then the reply generated by robot $i$ is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Reply}_{i \rightarrow j}^{t} \triangleq
\Bigl\{ \bigl(s,\Sigma^{+}\!\cap\!\mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi},
\Sigma^{-}\!\cap\!\mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi}\bigr) \,\Big|\,
(s,\Sigma^{+},\Sigma^{-}) \in \mathbf{Sense}_{i}^{t},\
(\Sigma^{+}\!\cup\!\Sigma^{-}) \cap \mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi}
\neq \varnothing \Bigr\},
\label{eq:task_driven_reply}
\end{equation}
where the set in \eqref{eq:task_driven_reply} is transmitted only if
$j \in \mathcal{N}_{i}^{t}$.
Equation~\eqref{eq:task_driven_reply} is the key filtering step. It removes all
propositions that cannot affect the recipient's local task and therefore keeps
the communication load aligned with the actual planning objective. Since the
filtered packet has the same tuple structure as a local sensing update, it can
be incorporated into the transition-system update rule without introducing any
new planning machinery.

The computation induced by \eqref{eq:task_driven_reply} is straightforward and
fully local. Each robot stores the subscription requests it has received, checks
which subscribers are currently reachable in $\mathcal{G}^{t}$, and filters
every fresh sensing tuple through the corresponding task vocabulary. Once the
resulting replies are delivered, the receiving robot updates its current
abstraction and invokes the history-aware revision step from
Algorithm~\ref{alg:history_revision} whenever the current continuation becomes
invalid or unsafe. Hence collaborative knowledge transfer does not replace the
single-robot adaptation mechanism; it supplies additional evidence that enters
that mechanism in exactly the same format as local perception.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Task-driven transfer and local revision at robot $i$}
\label{alg:task_driven_transfer}
\DontPrintSemicolon
\KwIn{Current abstraction $\mathcal{T}_{i}^{t}$, current run $\varrho_{i}^{t}$,
local packet $\mathbf{Sense}_{i}^{t}$, received packets
$\{\mathbf{Reply}_{k \rightarrow i}^{t}\}_{k \in \mathcal{N}_{i}^{t}}$}
\KwOut{Updated run $\varrho_{i}^{t^{+}}$}
Identify the current neighbor set $\mathcal{N}_{i}^{t}$ from
$\mathcal{G}^{t}$\;
\ForEach{$j \in \mathcal{N}_{i}^{t}$ that has not been contacted before}{
  send the subscription request
  $\mathbf{Req}_{i \rightarrow j} = (i,\mathsf{AP}_{i}^{\varphi})$\;
}
\ForEach{stored subscriber $(j,\mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi})$ with
$j \in \mathcal{N}_{i}^{t}$}{
  compute $\mathbf{Reply}_{i \rightarrow j}^{t}$ by
  \eqref{eq:task_driven_reply}\;
  \If{$\mathbf{Reply}_{i \rightarrow j}^{t} \neq \varnothing$}{
    transmit $\mathbf{Reply}_{i \rightarrow j}^{t}$ to robot $j$\;
  }
}
Update $\mathcal{T}_{i}^{t}$ using $\mathbf{Sense}_{i}^{t}$ and all received
packets $\mathbf{Reply}_{k \rightarrow i}^{t}$\;
Set $\varrho_{i}^{t^{+}} \leftarrow$
Algorithm~\ref{alg:history_revision} applied to the updated abstraction\;
\Return{$\varrho_{i}^{t^{+}}$}\;
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:task_driven_transfer} has two structural advantages. First,
subscription is persistent, so the task vocabulary of a recipient is announced
only once unless the communication graph disconnects and reconnects in a way
that requires re-subscription. Second, every transmitted reply is already
filtered by the recipient's specification. This prevents irrelevant semantic
information from propagating through the network and allows heterogeneous robots
to exchange only the facts that may alter their local automata or their local
workspace labels.

The following statement records the relevance property of the communication
scheme.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:task_relevance_transfer}
For any robots $i,j \in \mathcal{N}$ and any time $t$, every proposition
contained in a tuple of $\mathbf{Reply}_{i \rightarrow j}^{t}$ belongs to
$\mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By \eqref{eq:task_driven_reply}, every positive or negative proposition in
$\mathbf{Reply}_{i \rightarrow j}^{t}$ is obtained by intersecting the raw
sensing sets $\Sigma^{+}$ and $\Sigma^{-}$ with
$\mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi}$. No proposition outside
$\mathsf{AP}_{j}^{\varphi}$ can therefore appear in the transmitted packet.
\end{proof}

Lemma~\ref{lem:task_relevance_transfer} shows that the protocol is
task-driven in a precise sense: communication is restricted to information that
may affect the recipient's local temporal task. This property is important
because the communication graph can be sparse or time-varying, and the planning
architecture should not consume bandwidth on semantic facts that are irrelevant
to the current specification.

The next statement connects collaborative transfer to the correctness guarantee
already established for single-robot revision.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:collaborative_transfer_correctness}
Fix a robot $i \in \mathcal{N}$. Suppose every received packet
$\mathbf{Reply}_{k \rightarrow i}^{t}$ is sound with respect to the true
workspace semantics. Let $\mathcal{T}_{i}^{t^{+}}$ be the abstraction obtained
after incorporating local sensing and all received packets at time $t$, and let
$\varrho_{i}^{t^{+}}$ be the run returned by
Algorithm~\ref{alg:task_driven_transfer}. Then the projected run
$\rho_{i}^{t^{+}} \triangleq \mathrm{proj}_{S_{i}}(\varrho_{i}^{t^{+}})$ is
valid on $\mathcal{T}_{i}^{t^{+}}$ and satisfies
$\varphi_{i}^{\mathrm{hard}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The received packets have the same logical form as local sensing updates and
therefore induce the same type of modification of the local abstraction. After
all updates are incorporated, Algorithm~\ref{alg:task_driven_transfer} invokes
Algorithm~\ref{alg:history_revision} on the updated model. The conclusion then
follows directly from Theorem~\ref{thm:history_aware_revision}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:collaborative_transfer_correctness} clarifies the role of
collaboration in the present setting. Knowledge transfer does not couple the
robots through a joint temporal task, nor does it alter the hard-safety
guarantee established for the single-robot case. Its purpose is narrower and
more practical: it accelerates the refinement of each local abstraction by
routing task-relevant information to the robots for which that information can
change the current balanced plan.
