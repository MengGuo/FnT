\section{Case Study}
\label{sec:ch03_case_study}

This section illustrates the local-task formulation and the distributed
coordination mechanism developed in this chapter through a representative
heterogeneous multi-robot simulation. The objective is to show how
motion-and-action planning, collaborative request generation, reply evaluation,
local assignment, and online plan adaptation interact in a single execution,
rather than to introduce an additional planning layer beyond the framework of
Section~\ref{ch03:sec:task_relaxation_adaptation}
and~\ref{ch03:sec:dep-local-collab}.

%==============================
\subsection{Workspace and system description}
\label{ch03:subsec:case_workspace}

The simulation is adapted from the large-scale coordination benchmark in
\cite{guo2016task}. The execution statistics below are taken from the
reproduced run shown in
Figures~\ref{ch03:fig:case_execution_snapshots}--
\ref{ch03:fig:case_messages_over_time}. The benchmark setup is a
$60\,\mathrm{m} \times 60\,\mathrm{m}$ workspace with six task regions,
three storage areas, four base locations, and obstacles, as shown in
Figures~\ref{ch03:fig:case_two_layer}
and~\ref{ch03:fig:case_execution_snapshots}. The workspace is discretized into
$1.5\,\mathrm{m}$ grid cells, yielding a motion abstraction with $1600$ states
and $6400$ directed edges. The team contains $N=20$ robots partitioned into four
groups $g_k=\{a_{k,i}\mid i=1,\ldots,5\}$, $k=1,\ldots,4$: one UAV group
$g_1$ and three UGV groups $g_2$, $g_3$, and $g_4$. Each robot has a motion
model $\mathcal{M}_i$, an action model $\mathcal{B}_i$, and a complete
motion--action model $\mathcal{R}_i$ with states
$\langle\pi,\sigma\rangle\in\Pi\times\Sigma_i$, as in
Section~\ref{ch03:sec:collaborative}. The ground and aerial robots differ in
motion and action capabilities, while robots in the same group are homogeneous
except for their initial regions. Local actions take $5\,\mathrm{s}$, assisting
and collaborative actions take $10\,\mathrm{s}$, and the robot velocities are
chosen in the range $5$--$12\,\mathrm{m/s}$. The communication graph follows the
two-layer model used by the coordination protocol: local links are induced by a
$15\,\mathrm{m}$ proximity radius, and $a_{1,1}$, $a_{2,1}$, $a_{3,1}$, and
$a_{4,1}$ serve as group coordinators for mediated requests. Hence the
symbols \texttt{g1}--\texttt{g4}, \texttt{R1}--\texttt{R6}, and
\texttt{S1}--\texttt{S3} in the figures denote groups, task regions, and
storage areas, respectively, rather than preassigned collaborative partners.

The action and task models are capability based. Each UAV can execute a local
\texttt{record} action and a collaborative \texttt{circle} action; the latter
requires two assisting actions, denoted \texttt{hcirclea} and
\texttt{hcircleb}, from two other UAVs. Each UGV group $g_k$, $k=2,3,4$, has
three object classes $o_k^1,o_k^2,o_k^3$ stored in \texttt{S1}, \texttt{S2},
and \texttt{S3}. The first object class can be picked and dropped locally; the
second requires one assisting action from either a UAV or another UGV in the
same group; and the third requires two assisting actions from robots belonging
to two different groups. Representative sc-LTL tasks are given by:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\varphi_{1,1}
&= \Diamond(R_1 \wedge \mathsf{record}_1)
 \wedge \Diamond(R_2 \wedge \mathsf{record}_1)
 \wedge \Diamond(R_3 \wedge \mathsf{circle}_1),\\
\varphi_{2,1}
&= \Diamond(\mathsf{pick}_2^1
 \wedge \Diamond(R_2 \wedge \mathsf{drop}_2^1))\\
&\quad \wedge \Diamond(\mathsf{pick}_2^2
 \wedge \Diamond(R_4 \wedge \mathsf{drop}_2^2))
 \wedge \Diamond(\mathsf{pick}_2^3 \wedge \Diamond(R_6 \wedge \mathsf{drop}_2^3)),\\
\varphi_{4,2}
&= \Diamond(\mathsf{pick}_4^1
 \wedge \Diamond(R_3 \wedge \mathsf{drop}_4^1))\\
&\quad \wedge \Diamond(\mathsf{pick}_4^2
 \wedge \Diamond(R_5 \wedge \mathsf{drop}_4^2))
 \wedge \Diamond(\mathsf{pick}_4^3 \wedge \Diamond(R_6 \wedge \mathsf{drop}_4^3)),
\end{aligned}
\]
where the formulas specify what must eventually be recorded, circled, picked, and
delivered, but they do not prescribe which helper identities must provide the
assisting actions. Those helper identities are selected online through
\eqref{ch03:request}, \eqref{ch03:eq:reply}, \eqref{ch03:integer}, and
\eqref{ch03:eq:confirm}. Thus the LTL layer constrains the required temporal
ordering, while the dependence map $\texttt{Depd}_i$ and the communication
protocol determine the concrete collaborators during execution.

\subsection{Execution results and communication statistics}
\label{ch03:subsec:case_results}

All $20$ robots complete their assigned local tasks by
$t_f=156.2\,\mathrm{s}$. Over the full run, the simulator records
$N_{\mathrm{req}}=46$ coordination-request events. Among them, $15$ requests
are resolved through direct local-neighbor interaction and $31$ require
coordinator-mediated propagation. Hence approximately $32.6\%$ of all requests
are handled locally, whereas the remaining $67.4\%$ require broader
coordination. The run contains $40$ local actions, $46$ collaborative actions,
and $64$ assisting actions, for a total of $150$ executed actions. Thus the
average number of executed actions is $7.5$ per robot, and the overall
execution rate is approximately $150/156.2 \approx 0.96$ actions per second.
Moreover, the ratio $64/46 \approx 1.39$ indicates that a collaborative action
requires more than one assisting action on average, which is consistent with the
action-level dependency relation introduced through $\texttt{Depd}_i$ in
Section~\ref{ch03:sec:collaborative}.
Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_execution_snapshots} shows three representative
execution snapshots at $t=31.2\,\mathrm{s}$, $t=93.7\,\mathrm{s}$, and
$t=156.2\,\mathrm{s}$. The faded curves indicate the trajectories already
traveled by the robots. The snapshots show a progressive transition from early
deployment near the base locations, to the dense middle stage in which multiple
collaborative dependencies are active simultaneously, and finally to the
completion stage at which all local tasks have been satisfied. In particular,
the intermediate snapshot at $t=93.7\,\mathrm{s}$ shows several concurrent
dependencies across multiple regions, which demonstrates that the scheme does
not rely on a single global synchronization point. Instead, collaborations are
formed, confirmed, executed, and removed asynchronously according to the local
progress of the involved robots.

A more detailed view of the asynchronous execution structure is given in
Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_actions_timeline}. The horizontal axis denotes time,
and the vertical axis denotes the robot index used in the simulator log. The
labels \texttt{p}, \texttt{d}, \texttt{record}, and \texttt{circle}
denote pick, drop, record, and circle actions; labels prefixed by
\texttt{help\_} denote assisting actions executed in support of a
collaborative action. The timeline shows that collaborative actions are not
inserted by a centralized global schedule. Instead, they are requested when a
collaborative action enters the finite planning horizon and are executed at the
corresponding plan state together with the confirmed assisting actions. This
pattern is consistent with the
finite-horizon request mechanism of Algorithm~\ref{ch03:alg:request} and the
delayed adaptation procedure of Algorithm~\ref{ch03:alg:delay}: only the
affected plan segments are revised, while unrelated robots continue to progress
on their own local tasks.

The communication statistics further support this interpretation.
Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_messages_over_time} reports the number of messages
exchanged over time. The total number of exchanged messages is
$N_{\mathrm{msg}}=782$, of which $714$ belong to the coordinator-mediated route
and $68$ belong to the direct local-neighbor route. Hence the local route
accounts for only about $8.7\%$ of all messages even though it resolves
$32.6\%$ of all requests. Conversely, coordinator-mediated communication
accounts for about $91.3\%$ of the messages and $67.4\%$ of the requests.
Normalized by the number of requests, the average communication load is about
$17.0$ messages per request, including approximately $23.0$ messages for each
coordinator-mediated request and $4.5$ messages for each local request. The mean
message rate over the full run is approximately
$782/156.2 \approx 5.01$ messages per second. These numbers show that local
coordination is relatively cheap when it is available, while broader
coordination remains event-driven rather than persistent. The message bursts
therefore occur primarily near the instants at which collaborative dependencies
are requested, evaluated, and confirmed, which is precisely the behavior
intended by the distributed online coordination scheme.

Algorithmically, the instance also illustrates why the bottom-up construction is
used instead of composing the full team model. This comparison follows the
complexity estimates reported for the source benchmark in \cite{guo2016task},
and is independent of the reproduced completion time. The centralized product
for the same benchmark class would combine $20$ motion abstractions and the task
automata of all robots; using the reported UAV and UGV automaton sizes, this
would lead to approximately $1600^{20}\cdot 8^5\cdot 46^{15}
\approx 3.5\times 10^{93}$ product states. The distributed implementation
constructs products only at the single-robot level, where typical local product
automata are on the order of $10^5$ states and the reference implementation
synthesizes a local initial policy in about $2\,\mathrm{s}$ on average. Online
coordination is then restricted to the requesting action: the assignment problem
has $|\mathcal{N}_i|\,|\texttt{Depd}_i(\sigma_m)|$ binary variables for a local
request, and only the affected plan segment is delayed or revised when the
request cannot be confirmed. This explains the event-driven message bursts in
Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_messages_over_time}: the expensive global product is
avoided, and coordination is achieved  when a collaborative action enters a
the finite planning horizon of individual robots.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch03_summary}

This chapter developed a bottom-up view of multi-robot coordination
under local temporal tasks. Section~\ref{ch03:sec:task_relaxation_adaptation}
addressed task relaxation, safety-aware plan revision, online sensing,
and collaborative knowledge transfer in partially known workspaces.
Section~\ref{ch03:sec:dep-local-collab} then introduced dependent local
tasks with collaborative actions and a distributed execution-time
coordination mechanism for request, reply, assignment, and adaptation.
These developments show how correctness can be
maintained without a monolithic global mission, by coordinating locally
specified tasks whose interactions arise during execution.
