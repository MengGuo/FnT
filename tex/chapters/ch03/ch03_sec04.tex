\section{Case Study}
\label{sec:ch03_case_study}

This section illustrates the local-task formulation and distributed
coordination mechanism through a heterogeneous multi-robot simulation. The
purpose is to show how motion--action planning, request generation, reply
evaluation, local assignment, and online adaptation interact within the
framework of Sections~\ref{ch03:sec:task_relaxation_adaptation}
and~\ref{ch03:sec:dep-local-collab}.

%==============================
\subsection{Workspace and system description}
\label{ch03:subsec:case_workspace}

The simulation uses a $60\,\mathrm{m} \times 60\,\mathrm{m}$ workspace with six
task regions, three storage areas, four base locations, and obstacles, as shown
in Figures~\ref{ch03:fig:case_two_layer} and
\ref{ch03:fig:case_execution_snapshots}. The team contains $N=20$ robots in
four groups, one aerial and three ground. The notation \texttt{g1}--\texttt{g4},
\texttt{R1}--\texttt{R6}, and \texttt{S1}--\texttt{S3} denotes bases, task
regions, and storage areas. The setup induces spatial constraints,
action-dependent collaboration, and communication-mediated helper selection.
Each robot is assigned a local temporal task over the integrated motion--action
model of Section~\ref{ch03:sec:action-plan}. Collaborative dependencies are
generated online: when a collaborative action enters the horizon of
Algorithm~\ref{ch03:alg:request}, the requesting robot sends
\eqref{ch03:request}; helpers reply using Algorithm~\ref{ch03:alg:reply}; and
the requester solves \eqref{ch03:integer} before sending confirmations
\eqref{ch03:eq:confirm}. Thus the simulation directly instantiates the
request--reply--confirm structure of Section~\ref{ch03:sec:coordination}.
Candidate-helper search uses two message routes: direct local-neighbor
communication, and forwarding through group representatives when local neighbors
cannot provide the required assisting actions. This is only a communication
realization of the neighbor-query mechanism, not an additional centralized
planner; assignment and adaptation remain local. Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_two_layer}
shows one directly resolved event and one representative-routed event, both
following the same request, reply, confirmation, and adaptation logic.

\subsection{Execution results and communication statistics}
\label{ch03:subsec:case_results}

All $20$ robots complete their local tasks by $t_f=156.2\,\mathrm{s}$. The run
contains $N_{\mathrm{req}}=46$ coordination requests: $15$ are resolved by direct
local-neighbor interaction and $31$ by representative-mediated propagation. It
also contains $40$ local actions, $46$ collaborative actions, and $64$ assisting
actions, for $150$ executed actions in total, or about $0.96$ actions per
second. The ratio $64/46 \approx 1.39$ shows that collaborative actions require
more than one assisting action on average, consistent with the dependency map
$\texttt{Depd}_i$.
Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_execution_snapshots} shows snapshots at
$t=31.2\,\mathrm{s}$, $t=93.7\,\mathrm{s}$, and $t=156.2\,\mathrm{s}$. They
capture early deployment, a dense middle stage with concurrent dependencies,
and final task completion. The intermediate snapshot shows that coordination is
not driven by a single global synchronization point; collaborations are formed,
confirmed, executed, and removed asynchronously.

Moreover, a detailed view of the
asynchronous execution is provided in Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_actions_timeline}.
Local actions are marked by labels such as \texttt{p},
\texttt{d}, \texttt{record}, and \texttt{circle}, while \texttt{help\_} labels
denote assisting actions. Collaborative actions appear when the requesting robot
reaches the relevant plan state, and only the affected segments are revised;
unrelated robots continue their own local tasks.
The overall communication load is reported Figure~\ref{ch03:fig:case_messages_over_time}.
The run exchanges $N_{\mathrm{msg}}=782$ messages: $714$ through the
representative-mediated route and $68$ through direct local-neighbor
communication. Thus the local route uses only $8.7\%$ of the messages while
resolving $32.6\%$ of the requests. The average load is about $17.0$ messages
per request, with approximately $23.0$ for mediated requests and $4.5$ for
local requests. Message bursts occur near request, reply, and confirmation
events, showing that broader coordination is event-driven rather than
persistent.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:ch03_summary}

This chapter developed bottom-up multi-robot coordination under local temporal
tasks. Section~\ref{ch03:sec:task_relaxation_adaptation} treated task
relaxation, safety-aware revision, online sensing, and collaborative knowledge
transfer in partially known workspaces. Section~\ref{ch03:sec:dep-local-collab}
introduced dependent local tasks with collaborative actions and a distributed
execution-time mechanism for request, reply, assignment, and adaptation. The
result is a coordination framework that preserves correctness without a
monolithic global mission.
