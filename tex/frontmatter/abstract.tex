\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This monograph studies hybrid coordination for multi-robot systems that must
satisfy complex temporal tasks under coupled motion, action, communication, and
information constraints. Its purpose is to connect high-level formal
specifications with low-level robot execution in a way that remains
computationally tractable, operationally interpretable, and robust to the
structural difficulties that arise in persistent missions, including shared
resources, temporal dependence, heterogeneous capabilities, partial knowledge,
limited communication, uncertainty, and changing mission conditions.

The monograph develops a formal and algorithmic framework beginning with the
nominal single-robot setting, where weighted transition systems, temporal logic,
and automata-based synthesis are combined with hybrid execution. It then extends
this foundation through two complementary coordination views: bottom-up
coordination from local temporal tasks and top-down synthesis from global team
specifications. The framework is further generalized to communication-aware
planning, intermittent information exchange, human and online requests,
probabilistic task evolution, safe-return constraints, dynamic targets,
signal-based specifications, hierarchical abstraction, reactive replanning, and
continuous trajectory optimization.

The monograph shows that temporal-task semantics, symbolic abstraction, hybrid
execution, and scalable coordination mechanisms can be integrated within a
common framework for multi-robot systems in complex and evolving environments.
It clarifies how task decomposition, allocation, dependency management,
communication planning, topology adaptation, and reactive replanning interact
across nominal, communication-constrained, and uncertain settings. It also
identifies remaining limitations in scalability, nonstationary operation,
open-world reasoning, learning-augmented planning, runtime assurance, and
deployment evaluation.

The originality of the monograph lies in its unified treatment of hybrid
multi-robot coordination under temporal specifications across nominal,
distributed, communication-limited, and uncertain mission settings. Rather than
addressing task allocation, path planning, communication maintenance, human
interaction, and reactive adaptation as isolated problems, it presents their
methodological links through a common formal structure. This perspective shows
how symbolic planning, temporal decomposition, execution-time coordination, and
continuous motion synthesis can jointly support persistent multi-robot missions
with explicit correctness and operational constraints.
